Electronic and magnetic structure investigation of vanadium doped ZnO nanostructure

Abstract

In the present work, vanadium doped ZnO nanostructure, Zn1-xVxO (x = 0.00, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03) have been synthesized using sol-gel route. Rietveld refined X-ray diffraction confirms the single wurtzite phase for all the prepared samples with crystallite size ranging from 20 to 40 nm. UV-Diffuse reflectance spectroscopy and Photoluminescence (PL) measurement shows the decrease in band gap energy from 3.20 to 3.17 eV on V doping and strong visible emission region in the range 500–700 nm. PL broad peak in the visible region is further deconvoluted to study the defect states in the V:ZnO system. V doped ZnO nanostructure shows the room temperature ferromagnetism measured through Vibrating Sample Magnetometer hysteresis curve. The magnetic moment (μB) is found to vary from 0.012 to 0.018 μB per V-atom as x changes from 0.01 to 0.03 in Zn1-xVxO matrix. Magnetic structure properties are correlated with electronic transitions at different atomic levels using Near-edge X-ray absorption fine-structure (NEXAFS) spectroscopy. NEXAFS spectral features at OK-, V/Zn L3,2- edge reveals the hybridization of V(3d)-O(2p) orbitals and absence of V-metallic clusters in V:ZnO system. These investigations further envisage the existence of +5 state of vanadium in Zn1-xVxO nanostructure.

Introduction

Study of oxide-based magnetic semiconductors has evoked significant interest following their optoelectronic and spin-based properties for interdisciplinary scientific explorations. These materials have promising potential application in spintronics, which exploit both degrees of freedom of an electron i.e., charge and spin so as to fabricate fast and increased storage capacity electronic devices [1–3]. ZnO based diluted magnetic semiconductor have been investigated extensively because it has wide band gap of 3.3 eV at room temperature and large exciton binding energy (60 meV) [4]. Doping such semiconductor with transition metal (TM) often leads to modification in structural, optical and magnetic properties [5–7]. On the other hand, the electronic structure is highly influenced on doping of TM into the semiconductor material, as TM ions either substitute the cation or occupies the interstitial site in the host lattice and results into hybridization between p orbitals of host anion and 3d orbitals of the doped metal ion.
Eminent efforts are made to study TM doped ZnO system theoretically and experimentally for large Tc and high magnetic moment [6,7]. Vanadium (V) is one of the most interesting transition metal, which exhibits memory devices and sensing properties. It has small ionic radii when compared with Zn, thus band gap engineering can be carried out for V doped ZnO nanostructure for better sensor applications. Moreover, V doped ZnO system can be utilized as a flexible transparent ferromagnetic material at room temperature [8]. Theoretical predictions related to ferromagnetism in V:ZnO system was reported by Sato and Yoshida [9] but few experimental details were communicated in literature. Ishida et al. detected the small ferromagnetism in coexistence with paramagnetic and antiferromagnetic behavior in Zn1-xVxO thin films [10]. The Ferroelectric response was observed in two dimensional V-doped ZnO by Gupta et al. [11]. Wang et al. computed the results for V doped ZnO and believed that intrinsic vacancy played a crucial role in electronic density and magnetic moment [12]. Many other groups also claimed that defects/vacancies such as oxygen vacancy, zinc interstitial, zinc vacancy plays an important role along with magnetic ions in the origin of ferromagnetism [13–17]. Different models such as Ruderman-Kittel-Kasuya-Yosida (RKKY) interactions [18], Bound Magnetic Polaron (BMP) interactions [19], super-exchange, free-carrier mediated interactions [20] etc. are put-forward to understand the origin of ferromagnetism but still there seems to be an inconsistency in the reports on interpreting the origin and nature of magnetism in magnetic/non-magnetic ion doped semiconductors. In order to realize the ion interactions inside the system, experimental exploration of the electronic structure of V-doped ZnO nanoparticles is significant for user.
In this paper, we report the Electronic, Structural and room temperature magnetic properties of Zn1-xVxO (x = 0.00, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03) synthesized using sol-gel route. Structural measurements are performed using X-ray diffraction (XRD) and further their Rietveld refinement has been done. For optical study, Diffuse Reflectance Ultra Violet-Visible Spectroscopy (DRUV-Vis) is done followed by Photoluminescence (PL). The magnetic behavior is studied using Vibrating Sample Magnetometer (VSM) at room temperature. Local electronic structure properties are investigated through element specific and non destructive Near-edge X-ray absorption fine-structure (NEXAFS) measurements. The electronic transitions are further correlated with the origin of magnetism in V:ZnO matrix. These investigations are useful for spin based (spintronics), optoelectronic applications.
2

Experimental

2.1

Synthesis

Chemicals were purchased from Sigma aldrich and used as received. Zn1-xVxO (x = 0.00, 0.01, 0.02, 0.03) system is synthesized using Zn(CH3COOH)2⋅2H2O (ZnAc.) and V2O5 as starting chemicals. Stoichiometric amount of V2O5 solution was mixed to ZnAc. solution and final product was obtained using sol-gel route (described in earlier report [21]). The powder samples prepared are named as ZnO for x = 0, ZV1 for x = 0.01, ZV2 for x = 0.02 and ZV3 for x = 0.03 and these names are used thereafter.
2.2

Characterization

The crystalline nature of prepared samples is observed through XRD, Panalytical's X'Pert Pro (CuKα λ = 0.1541 nm) at Bragg angle ranging from 20∘ to 80∘. The lattice constant and reliability parameters (reveals the fitting quality) have been extracted by Rietveld refinement of XRD data using Fullprof [22]. DRUV-Vis (UV-2600 SHIMADZU) is performed to study the energy band gap. PL spectra (HITACHI F-7000) is taken at room temperature. Magnetic properties are investigated through VSM (Department of Physics, Himachal Pradesh University, Shimla). NEXAFS measurements at OK-, V/Zn L3,2- edges are performed on 10D XAS-KIST beam line, Pohang Accelerator Laboratory, South Korea in the total electron yield (TEY) mode using a chamber with vacuum of ≈ 1.5 × 10−8 Torr, respectively.
3

Results and discussion

3.1

Structure analysis

The phase purity and crystalline nature of ZnO, ZV1, ZV2 and ZV3 samples are studied through XRD measurements, as shown in Fig. 1. The diffraction peaks are indexed according to JCPDS No. 01-089-1397 with the appearance of three prominent peaks attributed to (100), (002), (101) planes, which corresponds to the systematic incorporation of V-ions at Zn-site in ZnO lattice. It further provides the evidence of emergence of single-phase wurtzite structure. All V:ZnO samples tend to follow a similar wurtzite crystal structure and thus have good crystallinity. To retrieve detailed crystal structure information, Rietveld refinement of XRD data is performed, as shown in Fig. 2. The XRD patterns analyzed through Rietveld refinement process are fitted using Fullprof [22] with space group P63mc. At first, scale factors and the background is refined and after that, the refinement of structure parameters such as lattice constant, asymmetries, profile shape and atomic positions is done in sequence. Possible oxides such as V2O3, VO2 are refined in order to check the presence of impurities or metallic clusters. In Fig. 2, the open circles correspond to the observed data and the solid line passing through them represents the Rietveld analysis result. The vertical ticks are the Bragg positions and below them, the blue line shows the difference between calculated and observed data. Quality of fitting has been examined by computing parameters like ‘goodness of fit’ (χ2), Reliability parameters such as profile factor (Rp), weighted profile factor (Rwp) and expected weighted profile factor (Re). The crystallite size after refinement is calculated by using Scherrer formula [21] and the values are listed in Table 1 along with various reliability parameters and lattice constant values. Usually for nano-crystalline materials, values of Rp, Rwp factors are large as compared to bulk materials [23,24], as also can be seen in Table 1. Moreover, the data is taken from lab XRD, so these values are expected to be high. However, the value of χ2 in all samples is less than 3, which implies the good matching between observed and simulated data and justifies the merit of refinement. The crystallite size decreases on V-doping and the lattice parameters a, c are found to increase and then remains almost same. Such variation is due to small ionic radii difference of V5+ (0.68 nm) when compared with the Zn2+ (0.74 nm) ion.

3.2

DRUV-Vis and photoluminescence study

The powder samples were taken for DRUV-Vis measurements to determine the energy band gap of V:ZnO system. Fig. 3 (a) shows the room temperature DRUV-Vis measurements for V:ZnO system. The Kubelka-Munk (K-M) relation (Eq. (1)) is applied to determine the consequence of V doping on the energy band gap of ZnO semiconductor.
(1)F(R)=(1−R)22R
here F(R) is K-M function and R is reflectance [25]. For all samples, graph is plotted between (F(R)hν)2 and hν as shown in Fig. 3 (b, c, d). The energy band gap value is acquired by linearly extrapolating the (F(R)hν)2 function to zero. The decrease in energy band gap value from 3.20 eV for ZnO to 3.17 eV for ZV3 is attributed to V-ion incorporation in ZnO lattice. Similar results of decrease in energy band gap value from 3.23 eV to 3.28 eV on V doping in ZnO as x changes from 0 to 0.08 in Zn1−xVxO matrix has been reported in literature [26]. Moreover bulk vanadium oxide has small direct band gap of 2.3 eV so it is quite reasonable to expect the decrease in energy band gap with the increasing Vanadium content in ZnO matrix. Thus V doping in ZnO matrix induces band gap engineering and the similar results with different dopants has also been reported in Wen et al. [27].
Fig. 4 displays the room temperature PL emission spectra of V:ZnO system with 320 nm excitation wavelength. The spectra is categorized into two regions; one in UV and other is visible region. UV transition between conduction band and valance band around 380 nm is assigned as near band edge emission that is attributed to the excitonic recombination. On the other hand, visible region consist of an intense wide and strong emission band covering the green-yellow-red visible range from 450 to 750 nm wavelength. These deep level emissions are controlled by synthesis route chosen and growth conditions. The broad emission band in visible region is further deconvoluted into three different peaks as shown in Fig. 5 (a, b, c). These emissions in visible region is due to bound excitons (bound to acceptor and donor defects such as structural defects, impurities, zinc interstitial (Zni), oxygen vacancy (Vo), oxygen interstitial (Oi) [28]. On excitation at wavelength 320 nm, the formed excitons partially lies in direct band gap and partially undergoes non-radiative transitions through donor states and defect states. Schematic representation of the mechanism of both UV and visible region in V:ZnO system is shown in Fig. 5(d). The band transitions at ≈ 570 nm is assigned to Zni and in the region 500–700 nm is due to Vo and [29].
3.3

NEXAFS study

Figs. 6 and 7 shows the room temperature NEXAFS spectra of V:ZnO system with various V concentrations at O K− and V L3,2- edge. NEXAFS marked the transitions of core electron to higher unoccupied orbitals following the dipole selection rules. In order to normalize the spectra, a straight line is subtracted before 514 eV and the intensity was adjusted to 1 at 558 eV. The NEXAFS spectral features are highly sensitive to nearest neighbors and local atomic environment, thus provides the detailed information regarding the hybridization of O (2p) states in conduction band with different sp and d states of TM [30]. For pure ZnO and V:ZnO system, NEXAFS spectra at O K− edge display various eminent spectral features from A1-A5 at 529.4, 533.8, 536.9, 539.5 and 542.7 eV, respectively (shown in Fig. 6). From these spectral features, useful information can be interpreted. The feature A1 is attributed to electronic transition from O (1s) state to empty O (2p) state, which is hybridized with V (3d) state. Since the d-orbital of Zn is fully filled so any changes in the spectra is caused by vacant V (3d) orbitals. The inflation of peak A1 with increasing V content is due to the increase in holes through O (2p) and V (3d) hybridization. The features A2 and A3 corresponds to the O 2p hybridization with Zn 3d4s orbitals. The higher region above 537 eV marked with A4 and A5 is assigned to O (2p) hybridization with Zn (4p)/V (4s) states and above this the features are due to overlap of O (2p) states with extended Zn/V higher orbitals [31]. Gautam et al. has also been reported the similar evolution of feature A4 in Co doped ZnO system and assigned them to oxygen vacancies [32]. The contribution from A1, A2 and A3 features is enhanced with doping concentration at 529.4, 533.8, 536.9 eV, respectively that revels the stronger s-p hybridization in V: ZnO system. Further it can also be elucidate that the spectral features above 537 eV are independent of V content in the V:ZnO system, and are dominated by the contribution from multiple scattering effects. Moreover the spectral feature A2 has been assigned to presence of oxygen vacancies in ZnO matrix, as reported in our earlier work [21] and the emergence of additional feature A1 is also allocated to the presence of oxygen vacancies and hybridization among O(2p) and V (3d) orbitals, which is supposed to be responsible for ferromagnetic nature of V:ZnO system.
Fig. 7 shows V L3,2 edge of V:ZnO system, marked with spectral features from B1-B4 at 515.9, 517.0, 518.2 and 525.0 eV. V L3,2 edge region shows a well resolved B1 and B2 features in addition to L3 and L2 edges at about 518.2 and 525.0 eV, respectively. These B1 and B2 are signature of electronic transition from V (2p) into empty V (4s) states [33]. Since in elemental vanadium, 4s states are fully occupied, the emergence of B1 and B2 features implies that vanadium is not present in elemental form in V:ZnO system down to the detection limit of XANES. In another way, no detectable metallic clustering of V ions/atoms is found in V:ZnO system. B3 and B4 features are due to transition of electron from 2p3/2 and 2p1/2 energy levels to V (3d)-O (2p) hybridized band. Furthermore, there is no notable shift in peak positions on varying the V concentration suggesting that the oxidation state of V remains same in all samples. Moreover the peak position of L3 edge suggests that V is in +5 oxidation state in the ZnO matrix [34].
Fig. 8 represents the Zn L− edge spectra of V:ZnO system at room temperature. The spectra is taken in TFY (Total Fluorescence Yield) mode and is normalized by subtracting a straight line below 1025 eV and adjusting the spectral intensity to 1 above 1080 eV. The peak around 1034 eV and 1037 eV can be assigned to hybridization between Zn (4d) (t2g) and O 2p orbitals and Zn (4d) (eg) and O (2p) orbitals [35]. The spectra provides confirmation regarding the hybridization among Zn (4d) and O (2p) bands thus supporting the O K− edge measurements.
3.4

Magnetic measurements

In Fig. 9, M-H hysteresis curve is identified at room temperature for V doped ZnO samples at different V concentration using VSM in the applied magnetic field of 1 Tesla with magnetization in units of emu/g. The hysteresis curves unveil the dependency of magnetization with the applied magnetic field for all V doped ZnO samples. The inset of Fig. 9 in the fourth quadrant display the diamagnetic behavior of pure ZnO nanostructure. Similar results for ZnO nanoparticles has also been reported in the literature [36]. For V:ZnO system, samples show a paradigm shift in magnetic behavior as a function of applied magnetic field. The measured magnetization curve is found to exhibit two magnetic signatures i.e. diamagnetic and ferromagnetic (not shown here). After eliminating diamagnetism, the hysteresis loop shows good room temperature ferromagnetism (shown in Fig. 9). At the same time, it is observed that saturation magnetization increases with an increase in V content in the ZnO matrix. The magnetic moment per V atom is calculated using relation (Eq. (2)) [37] and the values are listed in Table 2.
(2)μB=MwtMsNAγ
where μB is Bohr magnetron, Mwt is molecular weight of compound, Ms is saturation magnetization and γ is conversion factor and is equal to 9.27×10−21erg/Oe. As value of x changes from 0.01 to 0.02 in Zn1-xVxO matrix, it is noticed that μB per V atom changes by 0.002 number whereas from x = 0.02 to 0.03, μB per V atom shows a difference of 0.004 number. This might be due to small contribution of paramagnetic nature of ZV3. Tahir et al. reported the diamagnetic nature corresponding to V doping in ZnO nanoparticles in ambient atmosphere and observed the RTFM for samples annealed under reducing gas with maximum moment of 0.002 μB/V for 8% V doping in Zn1-xVxO matrix [26]. In the present case, the feasible source of magnetism thought to be the formation of micro vanadium clusters such as VO2, V2O3, V6O13 and zinc vanadium spinel ZnV2O4. Among these VO2 has non-magnetic nature whereas V2O3, V6O13 and ZnV2O4 exhibit antiferromagnetic behavior [38]. Therefore they are not contributing to ferromagnetism in V doped ZnO nanostructure. The only possible source that may cause magnetism is due to V clusters, however XRD measurements and their Rietveld refinement denied the formation of such secondary phases. Thus it is concluded that the observed ferromagnetism in V:ZnO system is an intrinsic property due to incorporation of V ions at Zn site in ZnO matrix.

Among the various exchange mechanisms, RKKY interactions [39] include the coupling between the magnetic ion and the conduction band electrons and this interaction is dominant in case of metals. However, V:ZnO system is not considered as metal at such low V doping concentration. The most accepted defect mediated mechanism to describe ferromagnetism in dilute magnetic oxides (DMO) is BMP. Along with magnetic dopants, oxygen vacancy plays a crucial role in understanding the magnetic origin in DMO [40]. The spin of dopant (V) incorporated into the ZnO matrix interacts through impurity band, which is formed by overlapping the low-lying triplet states from trapped electrons around the defects such as oxygen vacancy. Further, on the basis of observed strong visible band emission in PL and hybridization of O (2p) and V (3d) from NEXAFS measurements, we assume that BMP model (defect mediated (oxygen vacancy)) fits to understnad the cause of ferromagnetism in V:ZnO system.
4

Conclusion

In summary, we have experimentally reveal the existence of RTFM in V doped ZnO nanostructure carefully synthesized by sol gel method, and used the BMP model to understand the cause of ferromagnetism. Rietveld refine XRD confirms the formation of hexagonal wurtzite phase, thus marking the absence of secondary phase formation. DRUV-Vis studies indicate the decrease in band gap energy on increasing the dopant (V) concentration in ZnO. Along with UV-Vis, PL envisage the presence of defect states in matrix. Near edgeX-ray absorption fine structure measurements revealed the onset of V(3d)-O(2p) hybridization. Further, V L3,2 shows no signature of presence of metallic clusters of V atom in V:ZnO system. These investigations also exhibits the presence of oxygen vacancies as well as existence of +5 state of vanadium in these nanostructure.
