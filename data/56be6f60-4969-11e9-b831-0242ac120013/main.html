An examination of correlation between characteristic and device performance of ZnO films as a function of La content

Abstract

The aim of the study is to investigate the effect of lanthanum concentration on characteristics of ZnO films grown by chemical spray pyrolysis technique by varying doping ratios in the range, 0–5 at.% at a substrate temperature of 370 °C. For this purpose, microstructural, optical and electrical features of ZnO thin films were revealed as a function of lanthanum content. XRD measurements have revealed that all films had hexagonal wurtzite crystal structure without any secondary phase. Also, increase in precursor content has affected crystallization of the films as well as “a” and “c” lattice parameters and d spacing. It was proved by texture coefficient, standard deviation, strain and dislocation density calculation of synthesized films. Surface morphology of films has been evaluated via SEM (Scanning electron microscopy) and AFM (Atomic force microscopy) analyses and the results have supported that La content has led to mutate in surface morphology of the films. Optical properties of the films have been analysed by UV–Vis measurement. The optical band gap has been found to be connected with lanthanum doping and exhibit increasing tendency with a fluctuation. Electrical characterizations of Au/p-ZnO Schottky barrier diodes have been investigated taking current-voltage (I-V) and capacitance-voltage (C-V) measurements into consideration.

Introduction

Generally, ZnO is known as a versatile material in II-VI semiconductor group with a broad energy band gap (3.37eV), large exciton binding energy of 60 meV at room temperature [1]. In addition, ZnO has large research area from UV detectors to piezoelectric devices due to its unique properties like being nontoxic, inexpensive, high thermal and chemical stability at room temperature [2]. ZnO thin films can be obtained by using various deposition techniques including; pulsed laser deposition [3], chemical vapor deposition [4], sol-gel route [5] and spray pyrolysis [6]. Among them, chemical spray pyrolysis technique draws attention because of its inherent simplicity, inexpensive experimental arrangement and easy control of experimental parameters [7]. Also, quality of spray coated films is almost the same as those obtained by other expensive methods. Producing high quality films is especially important to design devices which have high efficiency. Literature surveys have revealed that doping is one of the most effective way to interfere with film quality [8,9]. Previous studies have verified that dopants like Al, In and Ga can enhance electrical and optical features of ZnO films [10]. For example, Yilmaz [11] has studied on sol-gel coated Ga doped ZnO films and observed that optical transparency of ZnO films improved with Ga content. Nomoto et al. [12] have also studied on Al doped ZnO films and results have showed that crystallographic orientation distribution improved with Al content. However, ZnO still has many problems and difficulties in practical application. Park et al. [13] have mentioned the difficulty of obtaining p-type ZnO films taking group I and Group V elements into consideration. So, it can be expressed that it is difficult to obtain homogeneous p-n junction since it has physical defects. For example, Mandalapu et al. [14] have studied on p-ZnO/n-Si heterojunction devices and they have concluded that there were difficulties in obtaining p-type ZnO. Their results have also showed that p-ZnO films could show a good photoresponse properties in both UV region and visible region if p-ZnO quality is controlled. Considered other studies [15,16], it can be said that rare earth (RE) elements are usually active elementsif RE ions are doped in ZnO films. Therefore, better quality and controllable ZnO films may be obtained. In this work, we have reported the preparation of high crystalline quality ZnO:La films grown by chemical spray pyrolysis technique. Furthermore, the effect of La loading on microstructural, optical and Schottky device performance of ZnO films have been investigated.

Experimental

ZnO an ZnO:La thin films were successfully grown on soda-lime glass and n-type Si substrates using chemical spray pyrolysis technique with a variation of La doping as much as 1 at.%,2 at.%,4 at.% and 5 at.%. Zinc acetate Zn(CH3COO)2⋅2H2O (Aldrich 99.9%) and Lanthanum(III) nitrate hydrate (La(NO3)3•XH2O) (Aldrich 99.9%) were used as ZnO and La source, respectively. Before using, glass substrates were cleaned with acetone, methanol and deionized water, respectively in ultrasonic cleaner. Also, using RCA1 procedure p-type Si was cleaned. ZnO:La thin films were formed by using an aqueous solutions containing 0.1 M zinc acetate precursors with few drops of HCl. So, pH of the solution was altered at 5. Then, an appropriate quantity of lanthanum was added in to zinc acetate solutions to achieve different doping concentration. (i.e., 0 at. %, 1 at. %, 2 at. %,4 at. % and 5 at. %). Obtained solutions were sprayed at a constant substrate temperature of 370 °C with a filtered compressed air as a gas carrier. Thicknesses of the films were determined by weight differences technique and all films were found to be at almost 350 nm structural and morphological measurements were taken by X-ray diffractometer with CuKα radiation (1.5418 Å), Scanning electron microscopy (SEM-Zeiss sigma 300) and Atomic Force microscopy (AFM-HITACHI-5110 N), respectively. Optical properties were evaluated by UV–Vis spectrophotometer (Schimadzu UV-3600 Plus). To analyze electrical performance of tZnO:La films, Au contact which had circular shape and 7.85 × 10−3 cm2 effective area was evaporated on ZnO:La films at 10−5 Torr pressure. Thus, Au/ZnO:La/n-Si/Al devices were fabricated.

Result and discussion

Fig. 1 has indicated XRD patterns of tZnO:La thin films and all films have hexagonal wurtzite crystal structure without preferred orientation because of (100), (002), (101),(102), (110), (103) and (112) diffraction peaks at various intensities. From Fig. 1, it can also be observed that there is highest reflection of peak intensities predominantly (100), (002) and (101) with low full width at half maximum (FWHM) for reference sample (ZnO:La 0%). This result supports good crystalline quality of reference sample. Similar XRD diffractograms have been observed by Salaken et al. [17]. As also seen from Fig. 1, (101) is the dominant peak for all samples. This case is directly associated with the substrate temperature and there is a reverse relationship between substrate temperature and (101) peak intensity. Namely, (101) plane's reflective intensity is enhanced by oxygen composition depending on lower substrate temperature and this composition decreases except from stoichiometry. On the contrary, c-plane orientation can enhance with oxygen rich composition [18]. Except from characteristic ZnO peaks, no different peaks based on lanthanum cluster, zinc or their complex oxides have been determined. This result supports that the films are single phase and La ions have dispersed homogenously in hexagonal crystal structure. As a consequence of La doping, the intensity of Bragg's peaks has continuously decreased up to 4 at. % La doping and then increased up to 5 at. % La doping content. The change in the energy absorption arising from physical or chemical interaction between La atom and Zn or O atoms during growth process can be shown as a reason for fluctuation in the peak intensity. This case was investigated in detail in our previous study [19]. Additionally, dominant peak of La doped ZnO has observed to shifttowards higher θ value. Actually, lattice distortion occurring due to the defects which are depending on vacancies, substitutions, etc. can be responsible for the shift in XRD positions. Similar results have been observed in Goel et al. for La doped ZnO [20] and they have told that the reason for this shift may be due to internal stress occurred by replacement of Zn2+ host ions by La3+ dopant ions. Furthermore, this shift supports the idea of La ion successfully doped in ZnO according to Vegard's law [21]. Kumari et al. [22] and Yilmaz [23] have reported similar shift towards higher angels for N doped and Ni doped ZnO films, respectively. Besides, we can see that XRD diffraction peaks shift to small angels with La doping in literature [24,25]. Lattice parameters “a” and “c” values as a function of different La content are calculated by a well-known formula [26] and results are given in Table 2. It is evident from Table 2 that variations of the lattice parameters “a” and “c” display reverse tendency with increasing La content. It is estimated that t change in the lattice parameters is due to the lattice mismatch between La3+ and ZnO lattice. (ionic radii of La3+, Zn2+ and O2− are 0.106 nm, 0,074 nm and 0.132 nm, respectively.) According to the calculated “a” and “c” values, decrease in “c” lattice constant value is relatively higher than increase in “a” lattice constant which is verifying the decrease in the lattice volume (V=32a2c) shown in Table 2. This result also supports above-mentioned reason on shifting Bragg's peaks to higher angles. Also according to ref. [27], presence of dangling bonds on the surface of ZnO films may be shown as a possible reason for lattice contraction. It is evident from Table 1 that calculated “d” values of samples are very close to its standard ones taken from JCPDS card no:36–1451. Reflection intensities are in harmony with preferential or random growth of polycrystalline crystal structure. Therefore, texture coefficient (TC) values can be calculated for each peak to determine preferential orientation of the crystal structure. In order to say that crystal planes have preferential orientation, TC values should be greater than unity. TC values are determined by following eq [28].
(1)TC(hkl)=I(hkl)I0(hkl)(1N)[∑NI(hkl)/I0(hkl]
where I(hkl) is observed relative intensity of a plane (hkl) and I0 (hkl) is the standard intensity of the plane (hkl) taken from JCPDS#36–1451.
Calculated TC values for each peak are given in Table 2 and demonstrated in Fig. 2 (a). In the light of these results, (101) has the most intense peak for all samples and (100), (002) and (100) planes have the highest TC values comparing with the other peaks. Also, TC (100) plane values gradually decrease with La content. This is a strong evidence that La ions enter ZnO crystal structure and lead to deterioration in the crystal structure. Generally, standard deviation (σ) calculations are done to determine nucleation type depending on high or low σ values. Eq. (2) is used to calculate it [29]:
(2)σ=[∑I(hkl)2−(I(hkl))2NN]1/2
where I(hkl) is relating relative intensity of (hkl) plane and N is tnumber of reflection peak. The variation of standard deviations with La content is given in Table 2 and Fig. 2 (b). From these, it can be said that σ values increase up at 1% La doping indicated heterogeneous nucleation, whereas σ values decrease with further La content shown homogenous nucleation. FWHM and crystallite size are generally used to estimate the quality of the crystal structure. Crystallite size, dislocation density (δ) and strain of c-axis (εzz) values of the films are obtained by using eqs (3)–(5), respectively [11,19,23].
(3)D=0,9λβcosθ
(4)δ=1D2
(5)εzz=c−c0c0x100%
In these eqs., λ is X-ray wavelength of 1.5418 Å, ϴ is Bragg's angle, β is the full-width half-maximum (FWHM) in radians, c and co are calculated (given in Table 2) and standard (taken from JCPDS≠36–1451) lattice constants, respectively. Crystallite size of the films are calculated by considering (100), (002) and (101) peaks and given in Table 2 and Fig. 3.
From Fig. 3, it can be observed that average crystallite size is almost constant with a little tendency to increase with La content. Lattice mismatch between films and substrate may cause varying degrees of stress during the growth process. Thus, strain can significantly cause structural properties of the films to change [30]. All calculated strain values of the films are shown in Table 2. Taking all above mentioned discussion into account, the tendency to decrease in “c” lattice parameters (and the strain of the films also decrease) given in Table 2 can be shown as a possible reason for the increase in average crystallite size. Similar results are observed by Benramache et al. [31] for spray coated Co doped ZnO films. Dislocation density can be shown another important structural factor for defining the measure of the amount of defects in the crystal structure. Lower δ value indicates higher crystallinity and lower structural defects. Considering calculated δ values represented in Table 2, it can be clearly seen that there is a tendency to decrease with fluctuations comparing with the reference sample.
Surface morphology of the samples are evaluated by means of SEM and AFM measurements. It is observed that the surface morphology ofZnO films depends on La content proved by EDS measurement given in Fig. 4. As seen in Fig. 4, there is no crack in the films and all films make up of pebble like nanostructure with observable grain boundaries; thus this evidence supports that all films have polycrystalline nature. Also, uniformity of the films improves with increasing La content (Fig. 4(a–e)). Similar surface topography have also been observed by the other researchers [32,33]. Fig. 4(b–e) shows the grains are larger compared to as-deposited ZnO and that may be due to the agglemoration of small grains depending on La content. Additionally, it is observed that surface changes in the film, where the sample formed cluster with large particle size at further La content (Fig. 4(d-e)). Similar observations have been made by Mkawi et al. for Al doped ZnO films [34]. They have also metioned that variation of the film surface occurs as a result of the formation of stress resulting from high ionic differences between Zn2+ and dopant atom in ZnO crystal structure. Taking the results in Table 2 into consideration, this can be seen as an acceptable for clarifying the variation of surface morphology with the incerasing La content. Similar surface morphology can be observed in AFM images given in Fig. 5. As seen from Fig. 5, the grains uniformly collect, interact with each other and these are visible as a completely compact. From XRD results of the samples, it is said that the grain size values exhibit increasing tendency with a small fluctuations with the increase in La content. Line scan AFM measurements of the samples shown in Fig. 5 support the suggestion. Also, having a suggestion on effect of La content on the uniformity of surface morphology, surface root mean square (rms) measurement have been acquired and the average rms value of the films is obtained. Rms values are 6.62 nm, 13.14 nm, 9.06 nm, 14.85 nm and 13.01 nm for 0%, 1 at. %, 2 at. %, 4 at. % and 5 at. % La doped ZnO films, respectively. Homogenous grain growth and differences of ionic radius between La3+ and Zn2+ ions which i leads dense in surface topography may be shown as possible reasons to explain these fluctuations in rms values. These suggestions are consistent with literature [26,35]., To sum up, all results obtained from AFM and SEM measurement are in harmony with each other and structural properties given in Table 2.
Optical features of La doped ZnO films are evaluated by means of transmission measurement in the range of 300–850 nm. Film thickness is a key factor of the optical transmittance. Thus, it should firstly indicate that all films have same film thicknesses at about 350 nm. Fig. 6 demonstrates the optical transmittance spectra of pure and La doped ZnO films. As ıt is clear in Fig. 6, all films have high optical transparency in the visible region. Additionally, films grown at different La content indicate a sharp ultraviolet cut-off varying from 355 to 380 nm corresponding to optical transition of ZnO because of the different film structure and stoichiometry. Similar comments have been made in literature [26,36]. According to Fig. 6, the optical transparency of the films is improved by La content except 4% La content. Variation in the optical transmittance of the films in the visible region attributes to the changes in either crystalline quality or surface roughness leading to more scattering of light. In this contex, it can be said that our AFM results are in harmony with the optical properties of the films. Namely, there is a reverse relationship between optical transmission and surface roughness of the films and this is the possible explanation to clarify the variation of the optical transmission between 4 at. % and 5 at. % La doped ZnO films. Similar observations have been made by Marouf et al. [37] for dip coated ZnO films. From the optical transmission spectra, it is possible to determine the value of optical band gap energy (Eg). For this aim, absorption coefficient (α) should be calculated by using following formula:
(6)α=ln(1T)/t
where T and t indicate optical transmittance and film thickness, respectively. Calculated “α” values obtained from Eq. (6), using the following expression in order to determine optical band gap energy (Eg) values:
(7)(αhυ)2=C(hv−Eg)
where hv is the photon energy, C is the constant and relates to electron-hole mobility and “α” is the absorption coefficient determined from Eq. (6). Eg value may be shifted to shorter or longer wavelength depending upon a number of factors given as follow:
  • i.
    The shift to shorter wavelength (blue shift) generally occurs as a result of the smallest particle size. If the particle size is smaller than Bohr's radius, the blue shift can be explained by the size effect or quantum confinement effect.
  • ii.
    If the particle size is greater than Bohr's radius, the change in Eg values may be due to the other parameters.
Fig. 7 exhibits the variation of (αhυ)2 vs. hυ with increasing La content for all films. The linear correlation of (αhυ)2 on hυ at higher photon energy refers that the films have direct transition electronic structure. When the straight-line portion of the curve is extrapolated to zero, it gives the optical band gap energy (Eg) values. Calculated Eg values are in the range of 3.19–3.25eV (Fig. 7). From these results, it is found that the Eg values shows the increasing tendency with increasing La content. Similar observations have been made by the literature for La doped ZnO films [38,39]. Additionally, some fluctuations are seen in Fig. 7. Namely, Eg value of pure ZnO rises from 3.19eV to 3.30eV with 1 at. % La contribution level then decreases to the values of 3.26eV and 3.12eV for 2 at. % and 4 at. % La contribution levels then rises again 3.25eV for 5% La contribution level. Rising in Eg value of pure ZnO film with 1 at. % La contribution level is possible result of Burstein-Moss effect (BM effect) which can be explained as follow:
ZnO is a well-known degenerate semiconductor and its Fermi level is going upper-side of the conduction band bottom (n-type semiconductor) or down-side of the valance band bottom (p-type semiconductor) with the increasing of dopant content. Namely, optical transitions occur between the valance band maximum and fermi level (for n-type conductivity) or fermi level and conduction band (for p-type conductivity). Narrowing of the optical band gap value with La codoping can be investigated taking two effects into considerations. First, La3+ ions will make additional electron states of impurity bands and they will place closer to the lower edge of the conduction band to form the new lowest unoccupied molecular orbital. This is the main reason for red shift of UV emission. Second, La3+ relocation can lead to strong sp-d exchange interactions between the band electrons and localized d electron of La3+ ions. s-d and p-d exchange interactions cause to a negative and positive effect on valance and conduction band energies, respectively. Similar results have been observed by other researchers for La doped ZnO nanoparticles [40]. Also, an increment in the optical band gap values for further La content (5% La) may be due to fragment of lanthanum oxide formation which has higher optical band gap value such as La2O3 is 5.5 eV in ZnO crystal structure. As a result of, that an additional energy level can be formed above the conduction band of ZnO because of the incorporation of La3+. Lan et al. [24] have also encountered with the same situation for La doped ZnO films. Similar variations in the optical band gap values with metal additives for ZnO semiconductors have been obtained by the other researchers [41–43]. Urbach band tail calculation may be carried out to get more information about the variation in band gap and the interband formation occurring as a result of La3+ co-doping. The optical band structure and optical transitions are influenced by the width of the available localized states in the band gap. Thus, the local defects are related with the band tail energy called as Urbach energy (Eu) and it is calculated by using following eqs:
(8)α=α0exphυEu
(9)Eu=d(hυ)d(ln(α))
where α0 is constant, hυ is the photon energy and α is the experimentally determinated optical absorption value. Eu values are determined from the slope of ln(α)vs.hυ diagram given as inset in Fig. 7. According to some literature [26,44], there is a relationship between Urbach energy and film quality. So, the decrease in Urbach energy indicates the increase in the film quality. Urbach energy values given in the study show fluctuation with an increasing tendency which is compatible with the ones obtained by XRD measurement given in Fig. 3. Also, Urbach energy values of the films change inversely with the optical band gap. Namely, both the optical band gap and Urbach energy value of the films are varied due to the broadening and narrowing of the band tail width because of the redistribution of states such as band to tail or tail to tail transitions. The conductivity type of ZnO:La films have been determined by using hot probe technique measured at 150 °C. Hot probe characteristics exhibit measured voltages as a function of heating time and show the similar features of a semiconductor regardless of its type. According to Fig. 8, it is concluded that only pure ZnO has n-type conductivity due to the exhibiting the positive voltage. The conductivity of ZnO has also changed with La doping because they show positive voltage indicating p-type conductivity. This is the reason why we use n-Si to fabricate of devices.
We have studied on applications of ZnO:La on n-Si as a Schottky diode devices. To this aim, it is necessary to use metal with a high work function for un-doped ZnO films to observe a good current-voltage (I-V) behaviour. For this purpose we used Al metal as an ohmic contact.
Fig. 9 shows I-V curves of all devices (hereafter the names of the devices are D1,D2,D3, D4 and D5 for ZnO:La 0 at. %, ZnO:La 1 at. %, ZnO:La 2 at. %, ZnO:La 4 at. % and ZnO:La 5 at. % based devices, respectively.) from Fig. 9, it can be stated that all devices have good rectifying properties such that the rectifying ratios of 2.0 × 103, 5.5 × 102, 2.1 × 102, 1.4 × 102 and 5.4 × 102 and the leakage currents of 2.2 × 10−9, 1.79 × 10−8, 2.0 × 10−8, 1.8 × 10−8, and 1.1 × 10−8 have been obtained for D1,D2,D3, D4 and D5 devices, respectively. Turn-on voltages of the diodes are determined as 0.2 V, 0.13 V, 0.12 V, 0.11 V and 0.10 V for D1,D2,D3, D4 and D5 devices, respectively. Generally, Schottky diodes have important properties such as quick switching and low turn on voltages compared to p-n junction. So, in order to get highly efficient p-n heterojunction Schottky diode, it is necessary to obtain devices having low turn-on voltage. Also, another important parameter for p-n heterojunction diodes is to have a highly conductive p-type layer with high holes concentrations. Similar observations have been made Turan et al. [45] for p-n GaN based diode. Above mentioned speculation is also in line with our C-V measurement. For ideal Schottky diodes, it is seen that the majority of the carriers are only carrying the current. To investigate I-V characteristics of a Schottky diode on a moderately doped semiconductor, it can be estimated that thermionic emission (TE) theory is the dominant current transport mechanism. So, only carrier having enough energy can pass over the Schottky barrier height and it can contribute to current flow. Therefore, mathematical formula of TE theory is given with I-V relation as following formula [46]:
(10)I=AA*T2exp(−qϕbkT)[exp(qVnkT)−1]
Eq. (10) shows non-linear I-V characteristics of a diode where Io defines as saturation current, given as:
(11)I0=AA*T2exp(−qϕbkT)
Io is obtained from the intercept of (ln I) vs. (V) curve on y-axis. A, A*, T, q, k,ϕb,V and n are defined as effective diode area, Richardson coefficient (112A/cm2K2 for n-type Si and 32 A/cm2K2 for p-type Si), temperature, electron charge, boltzman constant, effective barrier height, bias voltage and ideality factor, respectively. Thus, ideality factor value n is determined from the slope of forward I-V characteristics via the following eq.:
(12)n=qkTdVd(lnI)
According to TE theory, the ideality factor value equals unity for ideal diode. However, in this study, the ideality factor value is greater than the unity due to the various reasons. This case shows non-ideality diode characteristic and it may be due to the presence of an oxide layer between the junction materials, the recombination of the carriers in surface states, the generation in the deplation layer, high injection of carriers at large bias, the parasitic IR drop due to series resistance and the tunneling mechanism between states in the band gap of the semiconductor [47,48]. From our results given in Table 3, it is said that the ideality factor values exhibit decreasing tendency up to 4 at. % La content, indicating diodes approach the ideal, then it increases with 5 at. % La content. The variations in the ideality factor may be due to spatially inhomogeneous barrier height and potantial fluctuations at the interface between the junction materials. Similar results have been observed by the other researchers [49].

(13)eφb=kTln(AA*T2I0)
derived Schottky barrier height have been calculated and given in Table 3. It is observed that variations of the barrier height and nature of the ideality factor are the same with increasing La content. Shifting fermi level towards the center of the band gap with an increasing La content can be shown as a possible reason to decrease barrier height. This is also in line with our optical measurement given in Fig. 7. A reduction of the barrier height provides to convey majority electrons from semiconductor to metal. Wary and Rahman [50] have also mentioned about similar predictions for Sn/n-ZnO Schottky junction. Additionally, rectifying parameters such as ideality factor, barrier height and series resistance can differ from one device to another even if they have been fabricated under the same condition. The interface defects formed during contact fabrication may be shown as a possible explanation to clarify this case. As seen from the Fig. 9, the rectifying properties of the Au/ZnO:La devices depend on the bias and the effect of the series resistance is began to observe after +0.5 V. Namely, non-linear behaviour can be explained by means of the higher values of series resistance and this case is easily seen in insetted figure in Fig. 9. There are several techniques for determining the series resistance at rectifying contacts. We utilized Norde model to obtain experimental Schottky barrier height and series resistance values. The details about this model is given in eq. (14) [51].
(14)F(V)=Vγ−kTqln(I(V)AA*T2)
In these eqs, ɣ is an arbitrary integer greater than ideality factor (n), I(V) is forward current obtained from I-V data. To calculate junction parameters from Norde model, t F vs.V graph is drawn and then barrier height value is obtained from eq. (15)
(15)ϕb=F(V0)+V0γ−kTq
where F(V0) is the minimum point of F(V) vs.V plot and V0 is minimum voltage relating to F(V0). In the light of above mentioned information, F(V) vs.V curve is drawn and given in Fig. 10.
Experimentally obtained barrier height values are given in Table 3. When barrier height values obtained from eq. (13) and eq. (15) are compared, it is seen that the results are very close to each other with a small difference. The reason for small inconsistency of the barrier height values obtained from I-V and Norde model may be due to non-ideal I-V characteristics and barrier inhomogeneity of Au/ZnO:La Schottky devices. In summary, Norde model is more efficient in determining the parameters in diodes that are very close to ideal. In this contex, it can be said that if the ideality factor values get away from the ideal, the junction parameters calculated from Norde model can differ from TE ones. In other words, these parameters determined from TE or Norde model are identical for ideal Schottky device. Similar observations and discussion have been made by other researchers [52]. Additionally, differences between the value of barrier height calculated from Norde model and I-V curves support the presence of the series resistance and the interface states throughout metal-semiconductor. In Norde model, series resistance of the rectifying junction is given by eq [53].
(16)Rs=kT(γ−n)qI
series resistance values have been determined and given in Table 3. In general, Rs values have tendency of decreasing with fluctuations at some La content. Possible reason for the decrease of Rs with increasing La content may be due to grain boundaries. From the average crystallite results shown in Table 2, it can be concluded that the average grain size exhibits increase tendency with increasing La content. This means that an increase in crystallite size leads to decrease grain boundaries and less transport due to high series resistance values. This case is also seen in insetted figure in Fig. 9 as current limiting factor. This speculation is harmony with our results and crystallite size variation with La content given in Table 2. Yilmaz et al. [54] have also explained high series resistance by means of the grain boundaries for Ag/TiO2 nanotubes device.
Another way of calculating the barrier height is capacitance measurements determined as a function of bias. Fig. 11 indicates capacitance-voltage (C-V) plots of ZnO:La junction devices at various frequencies ranging from 100 to 1000 kHz. It is well known that C-V measurements are taken by superimposing an AC signal on throughout a DC biased device. In this study, we use 10 mV AC signal for this purpose. Moreover, it can be seen in Fig. 11, the low frequency capacitance increases with the bias, while the high frequency capacitance decreases. The charges presented in the deep states then only follow the changes in AC voltage toward low frequencies. In summary, the interface states can easily follow AC signal at low frequency but at higher frequencies, AC signal cannot be easily followed by charge at interface states. This case is also explained by considering stack capacitance showed in Sarswat et al.’s [55] study. According to their study, the capacitance of semiconductor layer given in Ref. [56] should has a low-frequency result. At high frequency, the generation ratio of the charge carriers from depletion region can not follow the rapid change of AC (applied signal) signal and thus observed capacitance has minimum value. This is the reason why the device capacitance has decreased with increasing frequency. Also, in forward biases, there are peaks limited the capacitance value. The presences of these peaks can be dedicated to the series resistance and interface states [57]. SBH is also determined by the capacitance-voltage (C-V) measurements. In Schottky diodes, the capacitance of depletion layer is given as [58]:
(17)C−2=2(Vd+V)/qεsA2Nd
where, A, Vd, εs and Nd are area of diode, diffusion potential determined from extrapolation of the linear C−2 vs V plot to V axis, permittivity of the semiconductor and doping concentration, respectively.
The value of the barrier height can be calculated by using eq. (18)
(18)ϕb=Vd+Vn
where Vn is the potantial difference between Fermi level and the top of the conduction band of ZnO films.
C−2 vs. V curves have been plotted taking 500 kHz frequency C-V measurements into consideration for all devices and depicted in Fig. 12. The value of C strongly depend on the bias. Some diode parameters ϕb, Nd, Vd, and Ef are calculated from C−2 vs. V curves and results are presented in Table 3. For all devices considered, ionized dopant densities exhibit increasing tendency with increasing La content. These are expected results for these kind of devices. Also, this can be seen as another reason of the decreasing optical band gap energy values with increasing La content. Similar results have been observed by other researchers [59]. Additionally, barrier height values have showed similar behaviour compared with the obtained values from I-V measurements. In this context, it is said that the barrier height values are smaller than ones obtained from I-V measurements. The possible reason for this unusual case may be due to the barrier heights obtained from I-V which is not influenced by the image lowering force. It can also be estimated that due to the wide band gap of ZnO (~3.2eV) and low doping of the epitaxial layer (˂1017cm−3), a significant effect on the barrier height from the image lowering force and recombination in the depletion region is not expected. Similar explanations have been made about tinhomogenous barrier height to clarify charge transport mechanism [60,61].
4

Conclusion

La doped ZnO films were successfully grown by simple chemical spray pyrolysis technique at 370 °C on n-Si and soda lime glass substrate. Their microstructural, optical and electrical features were analysed as a function of La content. It was concluded from XRD analyses that crystal quality of the films was deteriorated with the increase in La content. It was thought that this is the result of La3+ ions substituted Zn2+ ions in ZnO lattices. Variations in the band gap of ZnO films were explained with Burstein-Moss effect and sp-d exchange interactions between the band electrons and localized d electron of La3+ ions. Au/p-ZnO devices were fabricated and characterized by using I-V and C-V measurements. Diode characteristics like barrier height, ideality factor and series resistance were linked with the characteristics of ZnO films.
