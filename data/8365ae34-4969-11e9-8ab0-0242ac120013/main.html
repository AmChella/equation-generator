Influence of irradiation temperature on properties change of AlN ceramics

A.Kozlovskiyab*kozlovskiy.a@inp.kz
aThe Institute of Nuclear Physics of Republic of Kazakhstan, Astana, KazakhstanThe Institute of Nuclear Physics of Republic of KazakhstanAstanaKazakhstan

Abstract

The paper presents the results of studying the effect of irradiation of Fe7+ ions with an energy of 1.5 MeV/nucleon on structural properties of AlN ceramics at an irradiation temperature of 300, 500, 700 and 1000 K. The choice of ion type and irradiation conditions was made by studying the processes of defect formation as a result of particles effect with energies close to fission fragments of nuclear fuel (≤90–100 MeV) and temperatures 0.1–0.3 Tmelt, typical for annealing defects. From the data obtained by the XRD, SEM, EDX methods, it is established that an increase in irradiation temperature above 500 K leads to a reduction in distortions and deformation of the crystal lattice under irradiation. It was found that with an increase in the irradiation dose, amorphous-like grains with a high oxygen content are formed in the structure, which are characteristic for the impurity phase of Al2O3, the content of which in the ceramic structure does not exceed 5%. In this case, an increase in the irradiation temperature leads to a decrease in impurity phase concentration in the structure. It has been established that for samples irradiated at high temperatures 700–1000 K, the decrease in strength properties is less than for samples irradiated at a temperature of 300 and 500 K. Such a difference can be explained by partial annealing and annihilation of defects as a result of elevated temperatures. The decrease in thermal conductivity coefficient in irradiated samples is due to an increase in defects concentration in structure, as well as a decrease in their mobility.

Introduction

The development of nuclear industry and nuclear power requires the use of new classes of materials that meet high mechanical, thermal, insulation requirements, and must have high radiation resistance to various types of ionizing radiation [1–3]. One of the classes of new materials corresponding to these requirements are ceramic materials based on oxides or nitrides. Because of their unique physical-chemical, structural, thermal conductivity and insulating properties, ceramics based on aluminum nitride are considered as one of the promising materials of the reactor core of IV generation reactors [4,5]. One of the crucial conditions for the applicability of ceramic materials is their resistance to radiation and ionizing radiation [6–10]. Ionizing radiation can cause structural distortions, non-equilibrium and amorphous regions, capable of causing a significant change in the insulating and heat-conducting properties, may arise in it [11–15]. At present, there are numerous evidences of defects formation, and changes in their form, shape, speed, depending on the type of radiation and irradiation conditions [16–20]. The study of ionizing radiation effect is comparable in energy to fission fragments of uranium nuclei (≤90–100 MeV) on structural properties and operational characteristics of ceramic materials and has both scientific and practical significance. In this case, irradiation with heavy ions at temperatures from 0.1 to 0.3 Tmelt, characteristic for annealing defects in the structure, makes it possible to estimate the contribution of radiation defects to the change in the structural properties of ceramics, and also to simulate the conditions closest to reality. During irradiation at high temperatures in ceramic materials, point defects become more mobile, that leads to their accelerated migration along the structure and subsequent annihilation. However, an increase in the time and dose of irradiation leads to defects accumulation that does not succeed in annihilating each other, as a result of which regions with a high defect density are formed in the structure [21,22]. Moreover, modern theories of radiation defects formation [23–25] and their evolution in the structure do not allow to accurately predict the behavior of the structural properties of ceramics at high irradiation temperatures in connection with various competing processes in the structure. An increase in the irradiation temperature leads to a decrease in the binding energy of the atom in the lattice with its nearest neighbors. In this case, the volume of spontaneous recombination increases, which leads to an increase in the displacement energy of the atom. The increase in atoms vibrations in the lattice due to the increase in thermal contributions leads to the knocked out atom interacts less with surrounding atoms. That significantly affects defects recombination rate and cascades formation of secondary defects. Also, an increase in sample temperature can lead to the initiation of phase transformations in the material structure, which also has a significant effect on ceramics performance.
On the basis of the foregoing, it is interesting to study the processes of ionizing radiation influence on structural properties and defect formation in ceramic materials based on AlN. The paper presents the results of studying the effect of 1.5 MeV/nucleon irradiation with Fe+7 ions on properties of AlN ceramics in the temperature range 300–1000 K. This study is a continuation of a series of studies devoted to the study of ionizing radiation effect on structural properties of AlN ceramics performed on the basis of DC-60 heavy ion accelerator of the Astana branch of the Institute of Nuclear Physics. Previously, we established mechanisms of defect formation in ceramics as a result of irradiation by ions of Xe+22 [26] and Fe+7 [27] with energies close to the energies of fission fragments, but the effect of temperature was not presented in our earlier papers.

Experimental part

Samples of AlN ceramics were purchased from CRYSTAL GmbH (Germany), volume resistivity>1014*cm. The area of samples was 7 × 7 mm, the thickness of samples was 0.6 mm.
Irradiation of samples was carried out at the DC-60 heavy ion accelerator of the Astana branch of the Institute of Nuclear Physics with Fe7+ ions with an energy of 1.5 MeV/nucleon with a fluence from 1 × 1011 to 1 × 1013 ion/cm2 at an irradiation temperature of 300, 500, 700 and 1000 K. The choice of Fe7+ ion with an energy of 1.5 MeV/nucleon is due to the possibility of modeling fission fragments in reactors and its neutron irradiation. The main advantages are a relatively short set time of the required fluence, the absence of induced radioactivity and a large cascade effect of secondary products at irradiation. According to calculations of the maximum mean free path and energy losses of incident ions in ceramics made using the program code SRIM Pro 2013, the maximum mean free path of Fe7+ ions with an energy of 1.5 MeV/nucleon is 11.7 ± 0.5 μm, the radial deviation is 350 ± 20 nm, the number of vacancies created by one ion was 11100 ± 200 vacancies/ion. The energy losses of ions on electrons dE/dxelec = 10.08 keV/nm, energy losses at nuclei dE/dxnuclear = 0.018 keV/nm. For the calculations, the Kinchin-Pease model was used.
An assessment of the change in structural properties and defect formation in ceramic materials was carried out using the X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis method. XRD analysis was performed on a D8 ADVANCE ECO diffractometer (Bruker, Germany) using CuKα radiation. To identify the phases and study the crystal structure, the software BrukerAXSDIFFRAC.EVAv.4.2 and the international ICDD PDF-2 database were used. The mean free path of X-ray radiation in AlN material, at a tube power of 1000 W (Current 25 mA, Voltage 40 kV) is 50–60 μm. According to the calculated maximum ion path length in the ceramic and the X-ray path length, it can be concluded that the results of structural change measurements determined by the XRD method are measured both from irradiated and not from irradiated regions.
The structural characteristics before and after irradiation were investigated by a Hitachi TM3030 scanning electron microscope at an accelerating voltage of 5 kV.
The microhardness of investigated samples before and after irradiation and thermal annealing was determined using a digital microhardnessmer LM-700 (“Leco Corporation”, USA). The indenter was the Vickers pyramid. Microhardness (HV) was measured at a load of 1000 N.
The thermal conductivity was measured using a stationary method of measuring the absolute longitudinal heat flux proposed in Ref. [28]. The thermal conductivity was measured by the absolute steady-state method of longitudinal heat flow. The cross section of the sample was 5 × 5 mm, 600 μm thick, the distance between the temperature sensors was 10 mm. The thermal conductivity was measured for samples over the entire thickness of the sample, which included both irradiated and non-irradiated regions.
3

Results and discussion

The degree of radiation damage directly depends on the irradiation conditions (fluence, irradiation temperature) and the type of incident ions [29]. When irradiated in the crystal structure of the target material, non-equilibrium defects can be formed, which can have a significant effect on material properties. One of the methods of characterizing and evaluating the structure change under ionizing radiation is the XRD analysis method. Fig. 1 shows XRD patterns of samples irradiated with Fe+7 ions with an energy of 1.5 MeV/nucleon at a temperature of 300, 500, 700 and 1000 K.
The investigated initial samples are polycrystalline structures with a hexagonal type lattice of spatial syngony P63mc (186), which is an analog of zinc sulphide (wurtzite). Analysis of diffraction peaks revealed that in the initial structure, the presence of impurity phases of Al2O3-Triclinic of the spatial syngony P1 (1) is observed, in a percentage not more than 4.5% [26,27]. The presence of an impurity phase in the structure is due to technological processes for obtaining ceramic AlN samples. The determination of the volume fraction of impurity phases contribution was determined using equation (1):
(1)Vadmixture=RIphaseIadmixture+RIphase
Iphase is the average integral intensity of the main phase of the diffraction line (100), Iadmixture is the average integral intensity of the impurity phase, and R is the structural coefficient equal to 1.45. Fig. 2a shows the dynamics of the change in impurity phase concentration in the structure under irradiation.
An increase in the irradiation dose at a temperature of 300 K leads to an increase in the impurity oxide phase concentration in the structure of near-surface layer not exceeding 20–50 μm in thickness. In turn, an increase in the irradiation temperature leads to an insignificant decrease in the concentration of the impurity phase (no more than 5–7%). The decrease in the oxide phase under irradiation at high temperatures can be due to an increase in the thermal vibrations of atoms in the lattice at irradiation, accelerated migration, and subsequent annihilation of defects. In this case, the migration of defects in the structure has a significant effect on the distortion of the crystal lattice and the change in interplanar distances due to the migration of knocked out atoms and the formation of vacancies in the structure. Distortions occurring in the crystal structure as a result of irradiation can be determined using the Wilson formula [30] (2):
(2)lattice_strain=FWHMirr2−FWHMpristine24tanθ
where FWHMpristine, FWHMirr are the values of the full width at half maximum of the diffraction peaks before and after irradiation, θ is the diffraction angle. The results of the distortion calculations are shown in Fig. 2b. The magnitude of distortions of the crystal lattice of not more than 2% in the initial samples can be due to the presence of impurity atoms, which are located in interstices of the lattice and cause minor distortions in the structure. At small irradiation doses of 1·1011-1012 ion/cm2, for which the formation of individual point defects is characteristic, as well as the absence of ion overlapping regions, the magnitude of distortions does not exceed 3%. That indicates a high degree of radiation resistance of ceramics to irradiation. At the same time, according to the obtained data, an increase in the irradiation temperature leads to an insignificant decrease in distortions in the lattice. An increase in the radiation dose to 1·1013 ion/cm2 leads to a sharp increase in the amount of distortion (3–3.5 times). A sharp increase in the distortion can be since at doses of 1 × 1013 ion/cm2, the formation of clusters of overlapping defects and formation of a large number of defect cascades is observed in the crystal structure [31–33]. That can lead to the formation of small regions of disordering, as well as dislocation loops in the structure.
An important characteristic in the analysis of X-ray diffractograms is the width of the line of the most intense diffraction maxima, from the change in shape and area of which, we can estimate the contribution of various defects to the change in material properties. In this case, the broadening of the widths of the diffraction lines can be due to microstresses in the structure. That is associated with the accumulation of dislocations, as well as the crushing of crystallites related to recrystallization processes. An analysis of the angular dependence of physical broadening makes it possible to estimate the influence of both factors. To assess the effect, the Williamson-Hall method was applied, based on the relation (3) [34,35]:
(3)β2=Wsize2+Wstrain2,Wsize2=(λD⋅cos(θ))2,Wstrain2=(4⋅ε⋅tan(θ))2
where β is the physical broadening of the diffraction maximum, λ is the X-ray wavelength (1.54 Å), D is the crystallite size, θ is the Bragg diffraction angle, and ε is the value of the microstress in the lattice. As can be seen from the presented data, the broadening of the diffraction lines of the original samples is due to contributions, both from stresses and size factor. For samples irradiated at temperatures of 300 and 500 K, an increase in the contribution from stress is observed with an increase in the irradiation dose, which is due to deformation of the crystal structure as a result of irradiation. The increase in the contribution of stresses to the broadening indicates the formation of radiation-stimulated microstrains as a result of atoms displacement of their crystal lattice sites, the elastic and inelastic interactions of the incident ions with lattice atoms, and the formation of cascades of secondary defects. According to the calculated data of the SRIM Pro program, the main energy losses of the incident ions are due to losses on the electronic shells. In this case, the knocked out electrons can form cascades of secondary electrons, which migrating along the structure and can form regions of local heating along the trajectory of ion motion, according to the thermal burst model [34–36]. The formation of thermal bursts in the structure, and their subsequent relaxation with the formation of cascades of defects, can lead to an uncontrolled change in structural properties and a decrease in strength characteristics. An increase in the irradiation temperature above 500 K leads to defects high mobility and their subsequent recombination, which can lead to a change in the crystallite size and recrystallization processes. A smaller contribution of the stress at irradiation temperatures of 700 and 1000 K to the broadening of the diffraction lines can be due to thermal annealing of defects in the structure that compete with defect formation processes during irradiation. In this case, for all samples irradiated at a dose of 1 × 1013 ion/cm2, a significant decrease in the intensity is observed, which indicates a change in the texturization degree of ceramics.
An analysis of texturization degree of AlN ceramics before and after irradiation was carried out using the most intense diffraction peaks characteristic of the AlN phase. In the case of AlN ceramics, the most intense peaks are the diffraction peaks with Miller indices (100) and (101). The degree of texturization was calculated using formula (4):
(4)A=11+I100/I101+11+I100*/I101*1−11+I100*/I101**100%
where I100, I101 are the integral intensities of the irradiated (annealed) sample, I100*, I100* are the integral intensities of the initial sample. Table 2 shows the changes in texturization degree of investigated samples.

According to the obtained diffractograms, the measured relative intensity of the diffraction lines depends on six factors: adsorption, Lorentz factor, polarization factor (1 + cos22θ), structural factor F, factor P, indicating the number of texture planes in the sample and the temperature factor е−2М. Relative intensity is calculated by the formula:
I=F2P(1+cos22θsin2θcosθ)e−2M
The positions of peaks did not change on X-ray diffractograms and also new peaks did not appear. Therefore, the change in peaks intensity can be due only to the temperature factor. The fact is that the absorption does not depend on the angle, the structural factor does not affect the intensity since the type of structure did not change. Polarization and the Lorentz factor also do not contribute to the change in intensity. As a result of irradiation, the atoms of the crystal lattice undergo thermal vibration with respect to their location, while increasing the temperature leads to an increase in the amplitude of oscillations. An increase in sample temperature due to the energy losses of incident particles leads to an increase in thermal vibration. That has a large effect on the decrease in peaks intensity at large angles. As a result, peaks intensity decreases, and the intensity of background radiation increases, which leads to a reorientation of crystallites and a change in the degree of samples texturing. As can be seen from the presented data, with an increase in the fluence of irradiation for samples irradiated at a temperature of 300 K, a sharp decrease in the degree of texture is observed. This may be due to the formation in the structure of additional defects and disorder regions in the crystal structure. For samples irradiated at a temperature of 1000 K, a smaller change in the degree of texture is observed, which may be due to the partial annealing of radiation defects in the structure due to the increased temperature. One of the important crystallographic parameters for the hexagonal type of lattice is the ratio of the parameters c/a, the change of which reflects the deformation of the lattice under external factors. An increase in the parameter c/a indicates a deformation of the crystal lattice and a change in the interplanar distances (Fig. 3a). In this case, the change in parameters of the crystal lattice leads to a change in the volume of the crystal lattice and, consequently, the density of AlN ceramics. The density of the ceramic was calculated using formula (5):
(5)p=1.6602∑AZVo
where V0=32a2c is the volume of the hexagonal cell, Z is the number of atoms in the crystal cell, and A is the atomic weight of atoms. The results are shown in Fig. 3b. The integral porosity of investigated samples was found according to formula (6):
(6)Pdil=(1−pp0)*100%
where p0 is the density of the reference sample. The dislocation density (δ) contains information on the improvement of the crystal structure and is calculated according to formula (7).
(7)δ=1L2
where L is crystallites size. The results of calculations of the variation of the integral porosity, and the dislocation density in the structure under irradiation are shown in Fig. 3c–d.
An increase in parameters of the crystal lattice leads to an increase in lattice volume lattice, and, consequently, to a decrease in material density. The increase in parameters can be caused by migratory processes of vacancies, as well as the introduction of oxygen atoms from the impurity phase into the interstices of the lattice (the ion radius of oxygen is larger than the ion radius of nitrogen). The change in the density of irradiated ceramics leads to an increase in the integral porosity. That indicates the formation of disorder regions and regions with a high defect density in the structure. Also, an increase in the irradiation temperature leads to a smaller increase in parameters of the crystal lattice and ceramic density. That confirms the previously stated assumption of more active defect mobility and their recombination at high temperatures. The change in crystallographic characteristics (parameters of the crystal lattice, crystallites size, the dislocation density) as a result of irradiation can be explained using the model of thermal bursts arising as a result of the interaction of incident ions with atoms and target electron shells [37–40]. In this case, the obtained results allow one to estimate the irradiation temperature contribution to the change in the crystal structure and the processes of defect formation.
An analysis of the change in elemental composition of investigated samples, as well as the change in grain size as a result of defect formation during irradiation, is shown in Fig. 4. The measurements were performed using a scanning electron microscope (SEM) with an attachment for energy-dispersive analysis (EDA).
According to the EDX data, the presence of oxygen in the structure of initial sample of ceramics is observed in a percentage ratio of no more than 5%, which is confirmed by the results of XRD analysis. For irradiated samples, there are inclusions in the structure of the near-surface layer that differ from the hexagonal form of crystallites, which is characteristic of the hexagonal type of structures. With increasing radiation dose, an increase in the concentration of these inclusions in the structure is observed. In this case, the presence of these inclusions is observed only in the near-surface layer with a thickness of no more than 15 μm, which is confirmed by the results of calculations of the SRIM Pro 2013. According to the analysis of the dynamics of the change in grain sizes as a result of irradiation, the following conclusions can be drawn: an increase in the irradiation dose leads to a decrease in the average grain size, which is due to the processes of recrystallization during irradiation. The change in the grain size is shown in the insets of Figгку 4. In this case, the formation of grains with a high oxygen content is observed in the structure, which we showed earlier in Refs. [26,27]. An increase in the irradiation temperature leads to a decrease in the concentration of amorphous-like oxide grains in the structure. This confirms the results of a decrease in the oxide phase content determined by XRD analysis. According to the literature data [41,42], the formation of tracks and amorphous inclusions is not observed in AlN ceramics at irradiation with heavy ions with energy in the range 100–200 MeV. This fact is explained by the high radiation resistance of ceramics to ionizing radiation and the large amount of energy losses (over 30 keV/nm) necessary for the formation of tracks in the structure [41,42]. In connection with this, the formation of amorphous-like grains, whose average size is 70–120 nm, can be due to the presence of an impurity phase of Al2O3 and its amorphization as a result of interaction with incident ions (energy losses of Fe7+ ions in Al2O3 on electrons dE/dxelec = 30.07 keV/nm, energy losses at nuclei dE/dxnuclear = 0.056 keV/nm). Also, the formation of amorphous regions can be due to the introduction of oxygen atoms into the crystal lattice of aluminum nitride, leading to distortion and deformation of the structure.
One of the essential factors in assessing the applicability of ceramic materials is the stability of performance characteristics, such as strength, thermal conductivity, hardness and electrical resistance to radiation. In this case, the change in structural properties under the influence of ionizing radiation can lead to irreversible consequences of deterioration in the performance of ceramic materials.
The effect of irradiation on destruction of the crystal structure was estimated using a modified Griffiths criterion (8) [43]:
(8)Kcr=S2L
where S is the average stress, L is the crystallite size. The criterion of Griffiths characterizes the degree of structural failure and the formation of cracks in samples as a result of external action. Fig. 5a shows the graph of the dynamics of the Griffiths criterion as a result of external influences.
The change in strength properties can lead to a change in the hardness of ceramics and, consequently, to a decrease in the conductive characteristics (thermal conductivity and electrical resistivity). Vickers hardness for AlN ceramics was determined using formula (9):
(9)HV=0.0018544⋅Pd2
where P is the force (H), d is the length of the long diagonal of the print left by the indenter (mm). Fig. 5b shows the graph of the Vickers microhardness change due to external influences.
As can be seen from the presented data, an increase in the irradiation dose leads to a sharp decrease in the Griffiths criterion coefficient for samples irradiated at a temperature of 300–700 K. For samples irradiated at a temperature of 1000 K, the Griffiths criterion for small doses is reduced, which is due to a lower concentration of defects due to annealing in the course of irradiation. An increase in the irradiation dose to 1 × 1013 ion/cm2 leads to a sharp decrease in the Griffiths value. That is caused by the formation of disorder regions in the structure, a decrease in ceramics density, and an increase in dislocations concentration. Reducing the density and formation of disorder regions in the structure with a large concentration of oxygen can lead to the nucleation of cracks in the structure and uncontrolled destruction of the structure. In turn, an increase in dislocations density at high radiation doses can lead to the formation of dislocation loops in the structure, which lead to the formation of additional sinks of defects. Moreover, for samples irradiated at high temperatures of 700–1000 K, the decrease in strength properties is less than for samples irradiated at a temperature of 300 and 500 K. This difference can be explained by partial annealing and annihilation of defects as a result of high temperatures. The decrease in microhardness (Fig. 5b) can be explained by the formation of regions of structure disorder in the near-surface layer and a decrease in strength, as well as an increase in the porosity of the material due to the density change.
Thermal conductivity is a very crucial technological and operational characteristic of structural ceramic materials. The thermal conductivity was measured using a stationary method of measuring the absolute longitudinal heat flux proposed in Ref. [44]. The coefficient of thermal conductivity was determined using formula (10):
(10)λ=qδtc1−tc2
where q is the heat flux density, W/m2; tc1 and tc2 are the temperature constants on the hot and cold sides of the wall, K; δ is wall thickness, m; λ is the coefficient of thermal conductivity of the wall material, W/m⋅K). Fig. 5c shows the graph of the change in the thermal conductivity of ceramics as a result of irradiation. An increase in the irradiation dose leads to a decrease in the thermal conductivity, with the greatest change in the thermal conductivity observed for samples irradiated with doses of 1 × 1013 ion/cm2, at which overlapping tracks are observed, and hence the formation of disorder regions with a large content of oxide compounds took place (according to SEM and EDA). The decrease in strength properties and the density of material caused by the processes of defect formation and their subsequent migration leads to the destruction of ion and chemical bonds in the material that affect the material conductive characteristics. Fig. 5d shows the graph of the change in electrical resistivity of ceramics as a result of external influences. An increase in the irradiation dose leads to a decrease in the electrical resistivity of ceramic materials, which is due to a change in structural properties as a result of irradiation.
Therefore, the results obtained in the course of the study are in good agreement with earlier studies in this field [40–42] [45–47]], and can also make a significant contribution to understanding defect formation and radiation resistance in ceramic nitride materials.
4

Conclusion

The paper presents the results of the influence of the irradiation temperature on processes of defect formation and the change in operational characteristics (microhardness, heat and electrical conductivity) of AlN ceramics. The choice of the Fe7+ ion with an energy of 1.5 MeV/nucleon is due to the possibility of modeling fission fragments in reactors, and to determine the mechanisms of changing the structural properties and the radiation resistance of materials. Using the Williamson-Hall method, it was possible to estimate the influence of contributions of size effects and contributions from distortions on the deformation of the crystal structure. The results of XRD analysis showed that an increase in the irradiation temperature to 700 K and higher leads to a decrease in contributions from distortions and deformation of the crystal lattice to a change in the structural properties and the degree of texturization. A smaller contribution of the stress at irradiation temperatures of 700 and 1000 K to the broadening of the diffraction lines can be due to thermal annealing of defects in the structure that compete with defect formation processes during irradiation. The sharp change in the crystallographic characteristics and the of the disorder regions at irradiation doses 1 × 1013 ion/cm2 is due to the formation of overlapping regions of defects, and to an increase in the distortions and dislocation density in the structure. The formation of amorphous-like grains of size 70–120 nm is due to the presence of an impurity oxide phase of Al2O3 and its amorphization as a result of large energy losses of Fe7+ ions in Al2O3 (dE/dxelec = 30.07 keV/nm, dE/dxnuclear = 0.056 keV/nm), at that time as the energy losses of Fe7+ ions in AlN are three times less. It was established that for samples irradiated at high temperatures 700–1000 K, the decrease in strength properties is less than for samples irradiated at a temperature of 300 and 500 K. This difference can be explained by partial annealing and defects annihilation as a result of high temperatures. The decrease in microhardness and conductive characteristics can be explained by the formation of regions of structure disorder in the near-surface layer and a decrease in strength, as well as an increase in the porosity of the material due to the density change.
The results obtained are in good agreement with earlier studies in this field, and can also make a significant contribution to the understanding of defect formation and radiation stability in ceramic nitride materials, which are promising candidates for use in modern nuclear reactors.
