Miniature vacuum leak element made with porous nickel sheet

ChengxiangWangYanwuLi*andersonliyanwu@sina.comDetianLi**lidetian@hotmail.comYongjunWangYongjunCheng
Science and Technology on Vacuum Technology and Physics Laboratory, Lanzhou Institute of Physics, Lanzhou, 730000, PR ChinaScience and Technology on Vacuum Technology and Physics LaboratoryLanzhou Institute of PhysicsLanzhou730000PR China

**Corresponding author.

Abstract

Small characteristic sizes (<1 μm) for vacuum leak elements are essential to achieve low conductances and high operating pressures in the free molecular flow regime. In this study, a miniature vacuum leak element is fabricated using a porous nickel sheet with pore size of less than 1 μm and a vacuum coupling radius seal (VCR) type gasket. One side of the porous nickel sheet is well sealed for restricting conductance by the simple polishing and sintering method, and a hole with diameter of about 100 μm is drilled with a laser in the sealing film for conducting gas. In the molecular flow regime, the typical conductance of the leak element is ~10−9 m3/s, and the operation pressure can be up to about 5.0 × 104 Pa for Ar, and 1.0 × 105 Pa for He. Other advantages are: (1) the 4-VCR type leak element is very small, and can be easily fabricated; (2) the proper sintering process significantly improve the strength of porous material, and the presented leak is very robust; (3) the sheet shape for porous nickel is conducive to releasing the adsorbed gas; (4) technically, the porous nickel and the VCR nickel gasket can be welded together. Thus, the all-metal type leak is feasible.

Introduction

The use of vacuum leak elements is to limit gas flows or provide accurate flow rates, and are widely applied in vacuum metrology [1,2], vacuum leak detection [3], and gas sampling and analysis [4,5]. Some vacuum leak elements are obtained by drilling a hole in 1 mm thick metal sheets (stainless steel, aluminum, copper) with lasers [6]. These bakeable leak elements are simple, robust, and quite applicable. However, it is very difficult to get a laser-drilled hole with diameter of less than ~5 μm in 1 mm thick metal sheets. Much smaller sizes for vacuum leak elements are essential to achieve much lower conductances and higher operating pressures in the free molecular flow regime.
In the past several years, some new vacuum leak elements with characteristic sizes of less than 1 μm have been fabricated and investigated [7–13]. In 2009, Firpo et al. [7] fabricated ~200 nm orifices in silicon or silicon nitride (Si3N4) membranes using a focused-ion beam (FIB) technique. These nanometer orifices could work in the molecular flow regime up to atmospheric pressure. In 2014, Ierardi et al. [8] measured the geometrical sizes of the FIB nano-hole, and calculated the conductances by direct simulation Monte Carlo method. The conductances of a hole with diameter of ~200 nm and thickness of 200 nm were 5.25 × 10−12 m3/s for He and 1.70 × 10−12 m3/s for Ar. In 2015, Jousten et al. [2] found that the FIB holes clogged or the seal between the silicon chip and the copper holder broke in several cases. Therefore, they have chosen glass capillaries [6] instead of the leak elements with FIB holes for their measurement standard apparatus.
In 2016, Zhao et al. [9] made a leak element using the nanoporous anodic aluminum oxide (AAO) membrane. The uncovered area of 130 μm × 150 μm were obtained through sealing the unneeded nanotubes with a 400 nm aluminum layer, which was deposited thermally by the shadow mask coating technique. The diameter of nanotubes was about 70 nm, and the work pressure range in the molecular flow regime was up to atmospheric pressure for He, N2, and Ar. The measurement conductance of the leak element was about 8.55 × 10−9 m3/s (He), which was much larger than the calculated value of 3.85 × 10−9 m3/s (He). A 400 nm aluminum layer was not enough to seal the unneeded nanotubes well. In 2017, the optimized coating thickness was investigated by the same research group, and the 1200 nm aluminum oxide coating could perfectly seal the AAO nanopores [10]. Except AAO membrane, fluidic nanochannels [11] have also been used to make leak elements. Their main defect is that epoxy glues must be used for sticking (especially between the metal and nonmetal materials), thus causing problems like large absorption and desorption, and these cannot be easily solved by baking with very high temperature (>150 °C).
The all-metal elements can be bakeable, and more robust. In 2012, Yoshida et al. [12,13] used long cylindrical sintered stainless steel filters with a pore size of less than 1 μm to make all-metal leak elements, and good performances were obtained. After air exposure, a larger number of water will be trapped in porous metal materials, and it is very difficult to be completely released even after baking at 523 K for 15 h [14]. So we suggest that a porous metal sheet is better for desorption. In this work, a new miniature vacuum leak element is fabricated using a porous nickel sheet and a vacuum coupling radius seal (VCR) type gasket, and is investigated.
2

Experiment

2.1

Sintering of porous nickel

The porous nickel material (purity: 99.4%) is purchased from Beijing Jingming Powder Metallurgy Co., Ltd., and was made using particles with diameter of 2–3 μm through loosely sintering. Its pore size distribution is analyzed by the mercury intrusion method, and is mainly in the range of 100–800 nm (see Fig. 2 of Ref. [14]). Its porosity and specific surface area are 46.2% and 1.16 m2/g, respectively. The strength of this loosely sintered material is not enough, thus further sintering treatment is essential. The sintering process is implemented in a chemical vapor deposition (CVD) apparatus (OTF-1200X-III, Hefei Kejing Materials Technology Ltd., China). Firstly, the CVD apparatus is pumped to 10 Pa, and then the argon flow of 200 sccm is introduced during the next all processes. The heating rate is 10 °C/min, and the hydrogen flow of 50 sccm is inputted into the apparatus during the sintering process at temperatures between 550 and 925 °C for 1 h. Finally, the hydrogen flow is switched off, and the apparatus is cooled down.
Fig. 1 shows the SEM structures of porous nickel with different treatment temperatures. As shown in Fig. 1, at 550 °C the spikes on the surfaces of the particles will become somewhat rounded, and the surface changes are more significant at 675 °C. When the temperature is larger than 800 °C, the porosity reduces significantly, and the compacted structure with few pores forms. More details about the sintering of porous nickel can be gotten in Ref. [15].
2.2

Sealing layer and laser-drilled hole

First of all, the surface holes of porous material must be sealed for restricting conductance. The physical coating methods, including thermal evaporation [9] and magnetic sputtering [10], have been applied for AAO membrane. One well-known disadvantage of physical coatings is the bad adhesive force, especially when the substrate material is not well cleaned. In the presented work, we will try another simpler method for porous nickel. The black porous nickel samples before sintering treatment can be manually polished by sandpapers of 5000–7000 meshes (German Warrior Co., Ltd), and then have bright metallic luster, see Fig. 2 (a). Fig. 2 (b) shows its SEM image. As indicated in Fig. 2 (b), its surface is very smooth. The sandpapers of 80–2000 meshes have also been tried, and smooth surfaces cannot be achieved. Except the polishing process, the sintering process is also necessary to obtain good sealing layers. The reasons are: (1) the strength of the joints between nickel particles is weak. We have found that a large number of nickel particles would shed off by only a few minutes of ultrasonic cleaning. (2) In the sintering process, atoms will migrate and crystalline grains grow up. Thus, some micro leak paths maybe disappear. Fig. 2 (c) and (d) indicate the SEM images of polished samples after 1 h sintering treatment at 550–650 °C. As shown in Fig. 2 (c) and (d), large grains form and there are nearly no pores on the surface.
In this work, a YAG laser machine with focused beam diameter of 50 μm is applied. The wavelength is 1064 nm, the pulse energy is 0.6 mJ, the pulse duration is 120 ns, and the repetition rate is 20 kHz. Fig. 2 (e) shows the SEM image of surface crack formed by laser scribing on a polished sample. The crack region indicates that the thickness of the sealing film formed through polishing is in the order of 1 μm (one layer particles). The sealing property of this film will be analyzed later. A single laser-drilled hole can be used for conducting gas, and the conductance is limited and designable. Fig. 2 (f) presents the single laser-drilled hole in a polished sample. The sample is not sintered. As shown in Fig. 2 (f), the diameter of the influenced area is approximately twice the size of the focused laser beam. The thermal penetration depth is large, and the laser partially melts the material.
2.3

Fabrication procedures of miniature vacuum leak element

Fig. 3 indicates the assembling cross-section view of the miniature vacuum leak element. The fabrication process is as follows: (1) A standard blind nickel (or copper) gasket (NI-4-VCR-2-VS-BL, Swagelok Company, America) is drilled mechanically to get an orifice with diameter of 1.5 mm in its center. (2) Porous nickel material is cut mechanically into disks with diameter of ~20 mm and thickness of 1.0 mm. (3) The porous nickel disks are manually broken into square pieces with edge length of 3.0–4.0 mm. (4) One side of a square sample is polished by sandpapers, and then it is sintered at 600 °C for 1 h. Now, the strength of the sample is very high, and cannot be easily broken. (5) See Fig. 3, a piece of processed sample is glued on the center of a 4-VCR gasket using an adhesive (conductive silver paint, SPI Supplies, America), which is solidified through 0.5 h baking at 100–150 °C. The polished side of the sample is towards the 4-VCR gasket. (6) Finally, a hole is drilled at this polished side by laser using ten pulses. See Fig. 2 (f), a micro hole with diameter of about 100 μm is achieved at the center of the porous nickel sample. The depth of the hole is approximately 100 μm. It is evaluated with a cross-sectional SEM image of a sample, which is scribed with the same laser conditions.
2.4

Measurement setup

The schematic diagram of the measurement setup for the conductances of the vacuum leak element is shown in Fig. 4, and it is based on the constant volume and variable pressure method [6]. The measurement procedures are as follows: (1) all vacuum valves V1, V2, V3, and V4 are open, and the chamber (1 L) and the connecting pipes are pumped down through V4 for at least 1 h; (2) at the same time, the high vacuum system operates and high vacuum (<1 × 10−4 Pa) is achieved; (3) after pumpdown, the test gas is input into the chamber and the connecting pipes through V4, and then V4 is closed; (4) V3 is closed, and the upstream pressure Pu (see Fig. 3) of the vacuum leak element is given by the capacitance diaphragm gauge CDG2 (model: 698A13TRC, MKS Instruments, America; full scale: 133 kPa, uncertainty: < 1.0%); (5) the small decrease △Pu at time increment △t after closing V3 is measured by the capacitance diaphragm gauge CDG1 (model: 616A01TRE, MKS Instruments, America; full scale: 133 Pa, uncertainty: < 1.0%).
Thus, the conductance C of a leak at temperature T0 can be calculated by Refs. [9–11]
(1)C=−(V1+V2)ΔPu(Pu−Pd)Δt≈−VΔPuPuΔt
where Pd is the downstream pressure (see Fig. 3) of the vacuum leak element, it is less than 0.02 Pa in the presented experiment, and is negligible; V1 is the volume of the connecting pipes between leak, V2, V3, and CDG1, it is obtained with the static expansion method [16], and is 0.0716 L; V2 is the volume of a cylindrical chamber, and it is 1.0 L.
In the molecular flow regime, the pressure-independent conductance of a leak depends on the molecular masses and the temperatures of gases. If one gas's conductance at T0 is known (such as nitrogen), then the conductance of any gas with molecular mass Mn at temperature T can be calculated by Ref. [11]
(2)Cn(Mn,T)=C(MN2,T0)×(MN2/Mn)1/2×(T/T0)1/2
where MN2 and C(MN2, T0) are the molecular mass and the conductance of nitrogen, respectively. Eq. (2) is given with the assumption that the gas molecules are reflected from a solid surface with the Maxwellian distribution function. The surfaces of technical metals are inevitably contaminated after air exposure, and the results of Sazhin et al. [17] confirm that this assumption is accurate.
The conductance depends explicitly on temperature, see Eq. (2). The room temperature is controlled by an air conditioner, and the temperature differences between different measurements are less than 2 K. Then the relative change of the conductance according to the temperature term of Eq. (2) is less than 0.34%, and can be neglected. So, we only take Eq. (1) as the model equation for an uncertainty analysis. The implicit temperature dependence on geometrical size can be neglected, and the uncertainty of the conductance C is given by Refs. [2,11]
(3)u(C)/C=(u(V)/V)2+(u(Pu)/Pu)2+(u(ΔPu)/ΔPu)2+(u(Δt)/Δt)2
The measurement uncertainties of the capacitance diaphragm gauges CDG1 and CDG2 are less than 1%. However, because of the outgassing and the leakage of the experimental setup, u(△Pu)/△Pu is estimated to be 8–10%. The uncertainties on the measurements of volume V and time increment △t are about 1.5% and 0.3%, respectively. Then, the total uncertainty of the conductance C is approximately 8.2–10.2%.
3

Results and discussion

Fig. 5 shows the measured conductances C of the vacuum leak element. The measurements are implemented at room temperature (21.5–23.0 °C). As indicated in Fig. 3, in the measurement experiment the gas (He, N2, or Ar) at the polished side of porous nickel (upstream side) flows through the laser-drilled hole and the channels of porous nickel, and then goes to the downstream side. The upstream pressure is in the range of 0.9 × 103–1.3 × 105 Pa. As presented in Fig. 5, in the molecular flow regime the measured conductances of the vacuum leak element for He, N2, and Ar are 4.89 × 10−9 m3/s, 1.99 × 10−9 m3/s, and 1.56 × 10−9 m3/s, respectively. The measured conductance ratio of He to N2 is 2.46, it is close to the theoretical value 2.65 (see Eq. (2)); the measured conductance ratio of Ar to N2 is 0.78, while the theoretical value is 0.84 (see Eq. (2)). The relative deviations between the measured and the theoretical values are less than 8.0%. It can also be seen that the molecular flow regime is up to about 5.0 × 104 Pa for N2 and Ar, and 1.0 × 105 Pa for He. Yoshida et al. [12,13] made a leak element using the sintered stainless steel filter with a pore size of less than 1 μm. The upper limit pressure for molecular flow regime was about 104 Pa, which was lower than the presented results.
In Refs. [9] and [10], the physical coating methods were used to seal the unneeded nanotubes of AAO membranes. In the presented work, we have found that the polishing and sintering method is simpler and better for porous metals. The sealing property of the blind vacuum leak element (see Fig. 3, no laser-drilled hole) is analyzed using the measurement setup shown in Fig. 4 and the same experiment process for conductance measurements. The upstream pressure is in the range of 103–105 Pa, and the experimental gas is argon. The results show that △Pu/△t for the blind vacuum leak element is 0.3 × 10−3–1.0 × 10−3 Pa/s, while for the blind 4-VCR nickel gasket it is 0.2 × 10−3–0.7 × 10−3 Pa/s. Theoretically, △Pu/△t for blind gaskets should be zero. This measurement apparatus has been used for fourteen years, and a tiny leakage rate exists. According to Eq. (1), and taking Pu as 105 Pa, the conductance of the blind vacuum leak element is less than ~10−11 m3/s. It is at least two orders lower than the one of argon shown in Fig. 5. This result means that the polishing and sintering method can seal the surface pores of porous nickel well. In the sintering process, atoms will migrate and large crystalline grains form. The presented results confirm that the sintering process not only improves the material strength, but also significantly reduces the number of micro leak paths.
For all the reported leak elements made with FIB holes [7,8], AAO membranes [9,10], and nanochannels [11], epoxy glues were used for sticking and con-flat (CF) copper disks were taken for vacuum connections. It is well-known that the outgassing rates of several glues were large, and vacuum leak elements with glues could not be baked well. In the present work, we have fabricated vacuum leak elements using porous nickel and 4-VCR nickel gaskets. Technically, it is feasible to make all-metal bakeable leak elements with these materials. Since they are both made of nickel, and can be directly welded together. Furthermore, leak elements based on 4-VCR gaskets are much smaller than those based on CF copper disks. They can replace the common 4-VCR gaskets, which are widely used in vacuum systems. Thus, these 4-VCR type leak elements are smaller and cheaper, and can be bakeable ones.
H2O, CO2, and CO can be easily adsorbed on the surfaces of technical metals, such as stainless steel and aluminum [18–20]. Especially, water dominates (>85%) for at least ~10 h during the pump-down of unbaked vacuum systems after air exposure. It is because of that the sticking probability of water molecules on vacuum metal surfaces is very high, and the desorption is quite slow (long residence time) [21]. In addition, a large number of H2 can be solved in some metals [22]. In this study, the mass spectrum of a porous nickel sample is analyzed using the measurement setup for outgassing rates, which was reported in Ref. [23]. The diameter and height of the cylindrical sample are 22.6 mm and 61.8 mm, respectively. Fig. 6 shows its typical mass spectrum after 12 h air exposure and 2.5 h pump-down. As presented in Fig. 6, water makes up most of the desorbed gas, and other residual gases are CO2, CO, and H2. The results are similar to those of other technical metals [18–20].
See Fig. 5 of Ref. [13], the sintered stainless steel filter with long cylindrical shape was used for the leak element. In our previous work [14], after the water equilibrium pressure between the adsorption and gas phases reached 1.59 Pa, the porous nickel sample (diameter: 22.6 mm, height: 61.8 mm) was baked at 523 K for 15 h, and the released water was pumped 41 times. However, we found that the outgassing rate of water was still apparent. The characteristic time of desorption is approximately proportional to l2, where l is the characteristic size of porous metal [24]. Furthermore, given the large specific surface area (~1 m2/g), it is believed that smaller and thinner porous metals will lead to less adsorbed gas after air exposure, and most gas can be quickly released before use. Thus, the sheet shape is better than the block shape. The main benefit of long sample is lower conductance. In Ref. [13], the typical conductance was ~10−10 m3/s. In this study, the diameter of the laser-drilled hole is 100–110 μm, and the conductance for argon is 1.56 × 10−9 m3/s; while the exposed area of AAO membrane was 130 μm × 150 μm in Ref. [10], and the conductance for argon is 7.48 × 10−10 m3/s. Smaller holes will result in lower conductance. Zhu et al. [25] drilled a sub-10 μm through holes in thin metal foils with femtosecond laser. Especially, ~1 μm size through holes in a 30 μm thick copper foil were obtained by near threshold machining method. A 4-VCR type leak element with much lower conductance could be made using porous metals and femtosecond laser. This is our aim in the next work.
4

Conclusion

In this work, a miniature vacuum leak element is fabricated using a porous nickel sheet and a 4-VCR gasket. One side of the porous nickel sheet is manually polished by sandpapers of 5000–7000 meshes, and the sealing film with thickness of ~1 μm forms after sintering treatment. Its sealing property is evaluated to be good. Then, the sealing film is drilled through with a laser to obtain a hole with a diameter of about 100 μm for conducting gas. In the molecular flow regime, the typical conductance of the leak element is ~10−9 m3/s, and the operation pressure can be up to about 5.0 × 104 Pa for Ar and N2, and 1.0 × 105 Pa for He. The presented 4-VCR type leak element is very small and robust, and can be easily fabricated. In addition, the sheet shape for porous metal is conducive to release the adsorbed gas after air exposure. Thus, the presented miniature vacuum leak element may be a kind of very applicable one. Technically, it also can be all-metal type, which needs very advanced processing equipment.
