Multi-objective particle swarm optimization of EDM parameters to deposit HA-coating on biodegradable Mg-alloy

Abstract

The present research work is aimed to discuss a novel method of depositing hydroxyapatite coats, with interconnected pores, on the surface of bio-degradable MgZnMn alloy by using hydxroxyapatite (HA) powder mixed electric discharge machining (HAM-EDM) process. The parameters of HAM-EDM process has been optimized using multi-objective particle swarm optimization (MO-PSO) technique to determine the optimal levels of concentration of hydroxyapatite powder (CHA), peak-current (Ip), pulse-on (Ton), and pulse-off (Toff). The surface roughness (SR), thickness of recast layer (RLT), and micro-hardness (MH) were chosen as output response characteristics. The experiments were performed according to L27 orthogonal array and an empirical model has been developed to interpret the co-relation amongst input and output parameters. A number of optimal solutions (~100) were obtained by MO-PSO technique, where all responses were optimized. Among them, best optimal condition is to deposit biomimetic HA-containing layer with low SR (0.70 μm), low RLT (11.85 μm) and high MH (246 HV) was CHA = 5.28 g/l, Ip = 3.48 A, Ton = 40.33 μs, and Toff = 109.29 μs? Further, the specimens produced were characterized, morphologically and topologically, and revealed the existence of hydroxyapatite (HA) layer with interconnected pores of 5–10 μm size. The EDS spectrum showed that presence of Ca, P, and O on the modified surface, which conferred the deposition of HA-layer on the surface. The XRD pattern investigations of modified surface confirmed the formation of various biocompatible phases, which enhanced the mechanical properties, corrosion and osseintegration characteristics.

Introduction

During the last decade, researchers have been deveoped progresively bioabsorable/biodegradable materials for cardiovascular stenets and orthopadic bone fixation application such as plates and screws [1]. Magnesium as bioabsorable/biodegradable materials has been recived tremendous attention wordwide by virtue of the combination of low elastic modulus, high biodegradability and excellent biocompatibility [2,3]. The low elastic modulus effectively allivates the stress sheilding effect and the release of Mg ions accelarats bone ingrowth process [4]. However, pure-Mg degraded rapidly in aggerasive physiological environment (SBF or human body) and relased hydrogen, which deteriorate the mechanical-integrity of the implant before complete healing of bone tissue and implantation failed [5]. Therefore, till date, the unwanted consumption-rate of Mg is the predominant issue that restict the spacious utility for orthopadic fixtation inserts/accessories [6]. specialists have done loads of work to look for new techniques to improve the consumption obstruction. Over the years, researchers have done lot of work to enhance the corrosion resistance of Mg and its alloy by different methodologies such as surface modification by coating and microstructure improvement by element alloying [7,8]. Surface alteration by coating/deposition technqiue was found successful method that control the high degradation rate and protect the surface form corrosion [9–11]. Hydroxyapatite (HA) a bioceramic that has the ability to improve the corrossion resistance as well as biocompatibility of Mg-alloys [12]. Accordingly, numerous coating methodologies/techniques such as chemical conversion, micro arc oxidation, electrochemical deposition, anodization, ion Implantation, biomemetic, and sol-gel, have been used to deposit biomimetic HA-layer on Mg-alloy to improve its bioactivity and mechanical properties [13,14]. However, the weak adhesion of the coatings with substrate can lead to the deterioration because of the acidic body fluid [15]. Among numerous available coating methods, electric discharge machining (EDM) method is one of the simplest, effective, and economical processes to deposit biocompatible and biominetic layer on implant surface to enhance corrosion resistance, bioactivity, and mechanical properties [16]. However, the recast layer generated by EDM is generally hard brittle, and may composed cracks and pores, with tensile residual stress, which affects the fatigue and corrosion performance of the coating in intended applications [17]. So, number of advancements/hybridization has been done in EDM process to reduce the generation of surface defects and to improve the surface quality. Powder mixed EDM is one them and widely used the potential technique to deal with improve the surface quality by minimizing the surface defects [18]. PM-EDM was also used to modify the surface of Ti-based implants to enhance its biological, mechanical and corrosion-properties [19,20]. Recently, PM-EDM has been recognized as promising technique to deposit HA-based bio-mimetic on Ti-based implants [21,22]. Researchers have deposited HA layer on biomedical grade of Ti alloy through HA-powder mixed EDM in order to fortify the corrosion resistance and in-vitro bio-activity [23]. HAM-EDM process deposited a dense layer of biocompatible oxides free form defect on the machined surface in the form of recast layer, which has very high hardness and nono-porous in nature as compared to other conventional techniques. During the process, the HA layer filled the porous hole, gap, and voids, thereby improved the safety factor of the resulting implant. Thus, a thin recast layer free from micro-crack were deposited which improved the fatigue and corrosion performance of the implant in human body fluid environments. Moreover, the hydrophilic surface of the recast zone improved the various biomedical characteristics. As can be seen from the reported literature, PM-EDM/HAM-EDM process was extensively used to coat/deposit biocompatible and biomimetic layers that prevent the implant surface from corrosion and escalates osseointegration [24–26]. The PM-EDM/HAM-EDM process can be potentially used for the surface modification of bone fixation accessories (plates). Moreover, PMEDM was also used for the precise machining and drilling of other engineering materials [27–32]. Moreover, PM-EDM/HAM-EDM can be used to precise drilling of holes in the bone fixation accessories (plates). Moreover, in the past PM-EDM technique has been used as a finishing process and the effect of surface roughness on the fatigue performance of Ti-based implants was studied [33]. It was reported that PMEDM able to produce nano-level surface roughness, which enhanced the fatigue performance of implants. This is due to the fact that as powder particle dispersed in dielectric fluid, the discharge energy reduced. As a result of this micron- and submicron-scale debris were removed from workpiece surface and the roughness of the obtained surface is low near to micron and nano-scale.
The surface morphology and integrity of modified/machined surface depends upon the parameters of EDM/P-EDM/HAM-EDM process [24,25,34]. The surface morphology and integrity can be tuned by optimizing the process parameters. Therefore, optimization is very imperative to determine the best possible setting of input process parameters to maximize the response characteristics [19]. A number of single/conventional techniques such as Taguchi, response surface methodology, Grey relation have been adopted in the past to optimize the process parameters of EDM and PM-EDM process [35–40]. These strategies are single objective optimization techniques and were not all satisfactory because of the outcomes are clashing in nature. Non-dominated sorted genetic algorithm (NSGA)II was found suitable for the multi-objective optimization of EDM and PM-EDM process parameters to maximize the machine efficiency and to obtain quality surface [41,42]. Pathee et al. implemented the application of NSGA-II to determine the optimal setting of PM-EDM process parameters to maximize MRR and to minimize SR for EN-32 steel [43]. Recently, Mohanty et al. used the capability of particle swarm optimization (PSO) for the optimization of nano-Al2O3 mixed EDM process parameters for maximizing MRR and minimizing SR and TWR [44]. Kennedy and Eberhart presented PSO first time in 1995 an effective transformative computational procedure to optimize the response characteristics [45]. The PSO is developed to solve the consistent nonlinear streamlining issues after seen the behavior of natural swarm bird coming back to perch and observed in numerous types of winged creatures [46–48]. The PSO method can produce top notch arrangements inside short computation time and stable merging attributes [49,50]. Because of the great execution of the PSO system, numerous process engineers/scientists discover the PSO as a productive option over other inquiry calculations particularly when managing multi-target streamlining issues [51].
Till date, a limited work has described the modification of Mg surface via EDM/P-EDM/HAM-EDM process for biomedical uses [52–54]. Thus, in order to fill the gap, the major goal of this study is to investigate biocharacteristics of HA coated Mg sample processed by HAM-EDM. Moreover, the effect of individual HAM-EDM process parameters on the SR, RLT, and MH was studied. It was also observed that till date no one optimize the PM-EDM process paramters, to deposite biocompatible surface on implants surface. The application MO-PSO technique has been employed to pridcit the best optimal condition to optimize the response charectristc (low SR, low RLT, and high MH) of modified Mg-alloy.

2.1

Experimentation and characterization

In this work, MgZnMn based substrates were used for HAM-EDM machining. The surface of these substrates was finished and treated with ethanol. The HA particles with an average size of 100 nm were used as fillers of dielectric solution, refer Fig. 1. The experimental set-up for the surface modification was in-house developed and termed as “HAM-EDM process”, refer Fig. 2 for schematic description. The experimentation was performed in the specially-fabricated tank of 5 L capacity in die-sinking EDM machine (Electronica-5535). MgCa alloy rod (diameter 10 mm and length 50 mm) was used as an electrode, as reported in previous research study [55] (see Fig. 3).
From the pilot trials, four input process parameters are selected such as: CHA, PI, PD, and PC and the experimental conditions/operating parameters were presented in Table 1. Experiments were performed according to L27 Taguchi's orthogonal array, as shown in Table 2. Surface roughness (SR), recast layer thickness (RLT), and micro-hardness (MH) were chosen as output response characteristics. The SR, RLT, and MH observations were made in accordance of the reposrted literature [24–26]. Table 2 shows the obtained value of output response characteristics and their S/N ratios. The response surface modeling (RSM) was utilized to generate second order polynomial empirical model, refer eqn. (1), to establish the relation between various input process parameters with the responses. The equation described in the form as follows:

The MO-PSO method gives better execution when contrasted with the customary improvement strategy because of their heartiness, independency of slope data and utilization of inborn parallelism in looking through the plan space. Then, the characterization of the modified samples were done by using FE-SEM (JEOL 7600F) equipped EDS technique.

2.2

Multi-objective particle swarm optimization (MO-PSO)

Swarm Intelligence (SI) is a progressed dispersed smart model for understanding restricted and unrestricted optimization conditions that took its inspiration from the organic examples by swarming, shoaling and shoaling singularities [56]. Fundamentally, there exist only two optimization techniques which work on swarm intelligence: ant colony optimization (ACO) and particle swarm optimization (PSO). In the present research work, PSO has been selected for optimization. It is a populace based stochastic optimization tool which is formed by Kennedy and Eberhart, in a way to work on biological ideas specially inspired from the social conduct of bird flocking and fish shoaling [57]. The algorithm flow chart of MO-PSO algorithm is shown in Fig. 2. In PSO approach, the first word “Particle” signifies the individual element of flock that attributed on physical properties, for instance mass and volume. The word “Swarm” characterizes the variety of particles that are moving somewhere in large numbers for the optimization of the characteristics. The main key benefit of PSO over other global optimization algorithms is its fast convergence speed. Similar to other algorithms, it is started with a group of haphazard particles (solutions) in the solution space. The location of particle ‘I’ can be symbolized as in Eq. (2):
(2)Xi=(Xi1,Xi2,Xi3,………Xin)
It is must for each particle of swarm to memorize its previous best location that can be described in Eq. (3):
(3)Pi=(Pi1,Pi2,Pi3,………Pin)
Further, the continuous movement of each particle with its own velocity is calculated by Eq. (4):
(4)Vi=(Vi1,Vi2,Vi3,………Vin)
In iteration phase, the fitness function of particles records positions and best values are referred as personal best (Pbest). Subsequently, the best value calculated from the group is known as global best (Gbest), where the Gbest stores the best value of Pbest which could be achieved so far in the particular group only. The fundamental concept of PSO lies in the modification of location, a function of particle velocity, of the particle toward Pbest and Gbest locations. Velocity of each particle can be updated by using the concept of inertia weight approach, as shown in Eq. (5):
(5)vin=w×vin+c1×r1×(Pin−Xin)+c2×c2×(Pgn−Xin)
  • where, vin = velocity of particle
  • Xin = instantaneous location of particle
  • Pin = Pbest of particle i,
  • Pgn = Gbest of the group
Where ‘w’, ‘r1’, ‘r2’, ‘c1’, and ‘c2’ are inertia factor to limit the effect of prior velocity on updated velocity, random numbers used to reserve category of population within uniformly distributed interval [0,1], positive constant of the self-recognition t, and coefficient of the social component, respectively. On following Equation (5), particles choose their upcoming position, entirely inspired by own experience and the memory of its best previous location. The current positions of particles, in solution zone, are updated in accordance of [-s, s]. The following inertia factor (Eq. (6)) is usually utilized in:
(6)w=wmax−wmax−wminitermaxxiter
Where, wmax = initial weight, wmin = final weight, itermax = maximum iteration number, and iter = current iteration number.
Similarly, the current position can be modified by means of Eq. (7):
(7)Xin=Xin+Vin
The PSO is a computational technology wherein all particles in the swarm are inclined towards improved positions; hence the optimized solution can be attained via mutual efforts of the complete sample size. In the recent years, PSO algorithm has received all the attentions, of researcher community, businesses and enterprises and development sectors, owing to its easiness in building, effortlessness in application and competence in faster convergence.

Results and discussion

3.1

Effect of process parameters on SR, RLT and MH

Fig. 4 displayed the 3D surface response plot of SR, RLT, and MH with respect to input process parameters. Fig. 4 (a) presents the influence of CHA and IP on SR. The SR value first decreased with the powder concentration but after certain value of CHA (3–4 g/l), it started increasing. This may be due to fact that the dispersion of powdery material at concentration up to 3–4 g/l has decreased the thermal energy of the electrical sparks. This resulted in the formation of tiny craters on the substrate and hence decreased the SR values [18]. When CHA increased further above 5  g/l, it has been observed that the powder particles were stuck within the plasma and thus get deposited on the workpiece substrates. The SR was increased with IP, because at higher amplitudes of IP, larger heat was liberated and conducted by the workpiece. This increased the size of the craters, hence SR increased. The obtained findings are corroborative with the previous reported studies [18,26,33]. Fig. 4 (b) presents the influence of Ton and Toff on SR. The SR value increased proportionally with Ton, because it increased the discharge energy and thereby broad craters were formed. On the other hand, the SR decreased with an increase in Toff. At high Toff, the product of sparking got sufficient time to escape from machining zone before the subsequent sparks. The best optimal conditions to obtained low SR is A2, B1, C1, and D3. 3.2

Multi-objective optimization using MO-PSO

It is noticed that with a decrease in SR, the RLT and MH also reduced. Owing to the clashing nature of SR, RLT, and MH, it is important to locate the optimal set of conditions of input parameters that yield high MH, low SR and RLT. Accordingly, the function MH is now minimized and the resulting objective functions are as below:
  • Objective 1 = Minimize (1/MH);
  • Objective 2 = Minimize (SR);
  • Objective 3 = Minimize (RLT);
The present paper focuses on the optimization of HAM-EDM process parameters by implementing MO-PSO an intelligent optimization technique. The objective of this optimization study is to maximize the MH and to minimize the SR, the RLT with respect to the input process parameters, respectively. To perform the multi-objective optimization of process parameters i.e., HA powder concentration, peak current, pulse duration, and pulse interval with respect to SR, RLT, and MH there are requirements of some specified boundary conditions and fitness function to run the program. The details of boundary conditions selected for single variable optimization are discussed below:
  • 0 ≤ A ≤ 10 HA powder concentration
  • 3 ≤ B ≤ 9 Peak Current
  • 10 ≤ C ≤ 50 Pulse duration
  • 53 ≤ D ≤ 73 Pulse Interval
The same decision bounds, algorithm parameters and constraints are used for the multi-variable optimization of process parameters. In this method, initially the normalized fitness function is established by converting all the responses into single response. Then, the equal weights are assigned to the objective function as per their relative importance and the optimization is performed on combined objective value (X). This is to note that equal weights are assigned to all input variables and their sum was kept as 1. In this work, equal weight of 0.33 is assigned to both the response, as per Equation (11).
(11)Xmax=W1×X1X1max−W2×X2X2min−W3×X3X3min
Where,
  • X1max = Maximum value of micro-hardness
  • X2min = Minimum value of surface roughness
  • X3min = Minimum value of recast layer thickness
  • W1, W2, and W3 are the weights assigned to the responses, i.e., 0.33 for each response.
When the objective function is established, the next step of algorithm is to insert this objective function into the MATLAB code in the form of fitness function. After that, the program needs to be run and the optimized values are observed. Fig. 5 shows the convergence characteristics curve of individual output response characteristics and spread of Pareto front which contains 100 optimal set of conditions where all functions were optimized. It has been observed that MO-PSO predicted the best results within boundaries. Out of 100 global best solutions, first 5 solutions have been presented in Table 7.

3.2.1

Comparison of experimental and MO-PSO results for SR

In the investigation and comparing of experiential results of SR with Pareto front, it is observed that the minimum SR is 0.60 μm at HA powder concentration = 10  g/l; peak current = 3A; pulse on = 50 μs, and pulse interval = 150 μs, corresponding to experiment no. 21 in Table 2. On the other hand, the minimum SR obtained from the Pareto optimal front is 0.71 μm at HA powder concentration = 5.2  g/l; peak current = 3.48A; pulse on = 40.33 μs, and pulse interval = 109.29 μs?as shown in Table 3 (#1). The predicted SR from the optimal solution is slightly higher than the experimental value. Moreover, from the 3-D response of SR (Fig. 4), it can be clearly seen that the SR decreases if the HA powder concentration increases in deionised water and SR increases with peak current and pulse duration, indicated precision of experimental design. From Table 3, the maximum SR is 1.59 μm at HA powder concentration = 0 g/l, peak-current = 9A, pulse-on = 50 μs, and pulse-interval = 100 μs, corresponding to experiment #9 in Table 3. The maximum SR found from the Pareto optimal front is 1.59 μm. The results show that the maximum SR obtained from optimal solution is much lower than the experimental value.
3.2.2

Comparison of experimental and MO-PSO results for RLT

In the investigation and comparing of experiential results of RLT with Pareto front, it is observed that the minimum RLT is 6 μm at HA powder concentration = 5 g/l; peak current = 3A; pulse on = 10 μs; and pulse interval = 50 μs, corresponding to #10 in Table 2. The minimum RLT obtained from the Pareto optimal front is 11.85 μm, as shown in Table 3. The predicted RLT from the optimal solution is higher than the experimental value. Moreover, from the 3-D response of RLT (Fig. 2c–d), it can be clearly seen that the RLT decreases with the increase pulse-interval and HA powder concentration in deionised water. On the other hand, RLT increases with peak current and pulse duration. So, it indicates that proper selection of process parameter level gives the minimum RLT. From Table 3, the maximum RLT is 26 μm at HA powder concentration = 0  g/l; peak current = 9A; pulse on = 50 μs; and pulse interval = 100 μs, corresponding to #9 in Table 3. On the other hand, the maximum RLT obtained from the Pareto optimal front is 30 μm. The results show that the maximum RLT obtained from optimal solution is slight higher than the experimental value.
3.2.3

Comparison of experimental and MO-PSO results for MH

In the investigation and comparing of experiential results of MH with Pareto front, it is observed that the minimum MH is 134 HV at HA powder concentration = 5  g/l; peak current = 3A; pulse on = 10 μs; and pulse interval = 50 μs, corresponding to #10 in Table 2. The minimum MH obtained from the Pareto optimal front is 155HV, as shown in Table 3. The predicted MH from the optimal solution is lower than the experimental value. Moreover, from the 3D response of MH (Fig. 4e–f), it can be clearly seen that the MH increased with the increase in peak current, pulse interval and HA powder concentration. From Table 3, the maximum MH is 316 HV, corresponding to experiment #27 in Table 3. The maximum MH obtained from the Pareto optimal front is 246HV. The results show that the maximum MH obtained from optimal solution is lower than the experimental value.
Form the investigation of obtained results by single optimization; it was being observed that there was different optimal setting/condition for the individual output response characteristic. The SR (0.60 μm) minimized at (CHA = 10 g/l, Ip = 3 A, Ton = 50 μs, and Toff = 150 μs), RLT (6 μm) minimized at (CHA = 5 g/l, Ip = 3 A, Ton = 10 μs, and Toff = 50 μs), and MH maximized at (CHA = 10 g/l, Ip = 9 A, Ton = 50 μs, and Toff = 100 μs). There is no single best condition (set of condition), where all output response characteristic was optimized. So, MO-PSO provided one global best solution, where all output response characteristic was optimized. The best optimal condition to deposit biomimetic HA-containing layer with low SR (0.70 μm), low RLT (11.85 μm) and high MH (246 HV) is CHA = 5.28 g/l, Ip = 3.48 A, Ton = 40.33 μs, and Toff = 109.29 μs? Pareto optimal front also provide wide range of optimal solutions, it depend upon the decision of process designer/engineer. Moreover, depending upon the weightage of objectives in process or application of product, optimal setting can be tuned by varying the weights of individual output response characteristics.
3.3

HA-deposited surface characterization

Fig. 6 presents the morphology of modified surface of Mg-alloy by HAM-EDM process. Various micron-scale craters induced due to electrical-sparks were noticed on the both modified surface. Fig. 6 (a) presents the surface morphology of Mg-alloy at HA concentration of 0  g/l in deionised water (case of EDM only). From the SEM micrograph, high degree of surface defects like micro-cracks, rough surface, globules, redeposition of molten pool, and surface porosities was observed on the EDMed surface, which was a common observation in the EDM process [14,15]. The associated EDS spectrum of the EDM-modified surface confirms the presence of O element that indicated the formation of oxides on the machined surface. Fig. 6 (b) shows EDS mapping of the machined surface and distribution of various elements is visible. The existence of O element is primarily because of the deionization of water in the presence of plasma column. Fig. 6 (c) presents the surface morphology of Mg-alloy at HA concentration of 10  g/l in deionised water (case of HAM-EDM). From the SEM micrograph, the obtained surface was flat and smooth free form defects (micro-cracks, rough surface, and porosities), was observed. This is because during HAM-EDM process, the HA-powder particles decamped due to thermal energy and reinforced/redeposited on the workpiece surface in the form of recast layer. Thus prevents the formation of surface defects. Moreover, in HAM-EDM process, the generated thermal energy was lesser as compared to EDM, as results micro- and submicron-size craters were formed on modified surface and surface finish was good. The present findings are in-line with the published literature [17,20,21,55]. The associated EDS spectrum of HA deposited layer containing Ca, P, O, Mg, Zn, and Mn elements were deposited; refer Fig. 6 (c). The presence of Ca, P, and O confirmed the formation of biocompatible-phases, which improved the bioactivity of the surface. Fig. 6 (d) shows EDS mapping of the η-HA deposited surface and distribution of elements on the surface can be seen. Elements Ca, P, and O are commonly observed elements owing to the decomposition of the Hydroxyapatite. In plasma column, the HA was decomposed into Ca, P, and O elements that further formed a large amount of bio-compatible oxides phases.
4

Conclusions

The present study investigated the deposition of HA particles on Mg substrate via HAM-EDM processing. Further, the process parameters of HAM-EDM process were optimized by MO-PSO technique to observe the influence of process parameters on resulted SR, RLT, and MH of the samples. Followings are the brief conclusions that can be drawn:
  • •
    A biocompatible layer of HA has been successfully deposited on Mg alloy to enhance its surface integrity, mechanical properties, and corrosion resistance.
  • •
    The SR and RLT increased with the increase in Ip and Ton and decreased with CHA and Ton. The HA deposition on the Mg-substrate was increased with Ip and CHA. The MH increased with the increase in Ip, Ton, and Toff.
  • •
    The Taguchi's methodology integrated with response surface methodology to develop mathematical model for individual output response characteristics (SR, RLT, and MH). The relationship between input process parameters of HAM-EDM to output response characteristics is non-linear. The ANOVA (p-value < = 0.05) has been carried out and it has been found that the all process parameters of HAM-EDM process were statistically significant for SR. For RLT, all process parameters were statistically significant except interaction of Ip × CHA and CHA × Ton. For MH, all process parameters were statistically significant except interaction of Ip × CHA.
  • •
    The MO-PSO results revealed that best optimal condition to deposit biomimetic HA-containing layer with low SR (0.70 μm), low RLT (11.85 μm) and high MH (246 HV) was CHA = 5.28 g/l, Ip = 3.48 A, Ton = 40.33 μs, and Toff = 109.29 μs?
  • •
    The developed discharge energy due to electrical spark has influenced the chemical-composition of the MgZnMn alloy and high intensity of Ca, P, and O along with base material elements (Mg, Zn, and Mn) confirmed the deposition of dense and thick layer of HA.
  • •
    The XRD pattern confirmed the formation of various phases (such as CaMg, MgZn, MnCaO, MnP, and CaMnO) that improved the MH values to about of untreated base substrate times in comparison of base substrate.
