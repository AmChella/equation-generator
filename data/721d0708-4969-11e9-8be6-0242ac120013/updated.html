The variation of microstructures, textures and mechanical properties from edge to center in cross section of Ti6242s titanium alloy

GuomingZhenga*zgming0409@sina.comXiaonanMaoabLeiLibRuiDangb
aSchool of Materials Science and Engineering, Northeastern University, ShenYang, 110006, ChinaSchool of Materials Science and EngineeringNortheastern UniversityShenYang110006China
bNorthwest Institute for Nonferrous Metal Research, Xi'an, 710016, ChinaNorthwest Institute for Nonferrous Metal ResearchXi'an710016China
*Corresponding author.

Abstract

Microstructures and textures determine the mechanical properties of metal materials. In this work, a Ti6242S bar with ϕ260*129 mm scale was selected to study variation of microstructures and textures from edge to center by Optical Microscope and X-Ray Diffraction. Owing to the less plane stress, fast cooling rate and complex flow stress, grains size are quietly fine and macrotextures are close to nearly random in edge, which lead to the most excellent and homogeneous mechanical properties. By comparing feature of microstructure in R/2 and center position, larger volume fraction of αs colonies formed in center. Meanwhile, the main texture components were (112¯0) [11¯00] and (112¯0)[0001] in R/2, but symmetrical cubic texture with very high intensity come into being in center. More and larger αs colonies combine high-density texture jointly cause tensile strength and yield strength drop dramatically in center. Young's modulus is mainly influenced by textures, due to different texture in different location, edge was the largest, following by the center and R/2. The spheroidization of αp laths was completed in edge by strain-induced but dynamic recrystallization in R/2 and center by analyzing grain boundary feature and misorientation of EBSD data.

In addition, symmetrical cubic texture with maximum intensity of 7.9 times random is formed, which combine to led to tensile and yield strength drop quickly.

Introduction

As well known, Microstructures and textures determine the mechanical properties of a material [1]. Therefore, it is highly recommended for material designers to well understand relationship between them. Owing to the high strength to weight ratio, titanium and its alloys are important materials in jet engines [2–4]. It can be classified into five groups depending on the concentration of alloying elements, which have a significant influence on the β-transus temperature. Among various titanium alloys, the near-α titanium alloys are often used as compressor discs and blades due to the advances of superior fatigue and creep properties [5,6].
Processing near-α titanium alloys included several stages, such as forging above β-transus, subtransus hot-processing and heat treatments in suitable temperature, each stage had special microstructure goals [1]. In recent years, the aviation industry continues to demand more excellent material performance constantly. In order to obtain desirable mechanical properties, a good match of microstructure and texture must be optimized. Generally, getting a uniform microstructure as well as random texture is always popular. However, it is noticed that for a big scale stock or disk, the microstructures and textures make a significant difference from edge to center because of the changes in deformation condition. As a result, mechanical properties vary greatly in different position, which usually lead to material failure in practical applications. Gay [7] investigated the variations of microtextures in a forged disk of IMI834, he found that the difference of plane stress distribution and cooling rate in different positions lead to inhomogeneity microtextures distribution. Pilchak [8] also discovered that the microstructures, textures, and microtextures changed significantly from different location in different diameters Ti6242S bar. It is no doubt that these changes can rise the heterogeneity of mechanical properties of titanium alloys. Unfortunately, in terms of existing production technologies, it is very difficult to produce titanium alloys with uniform structure and random texture. As a practical matter, in order to reduce accidents caused by inhomogeneity of mechanical properties of materials as soon as possible, an effective method is to predict the evolution of microstructure and texture of materials and further estimate its service life, replacing old materials with new materials in time before the failure of materials. For this purpose, according to plane stress strategy, even few specialists [9,10] established processing maps of the billet to predict the development of microstructures and textures, so that it provide better service for industry manufacture. However, so far, these models are defective and there is no effective model to predict evolution structures and textures of for big scale stock at different positions, especially for forecast of texture. Because of the detriments of inhomogeneity of mechanical properties in different position of the titanium alloys bar, it is quietly necessary to make clear the evolution of microstructure and texture and the corresponding mechanical properties. Nevertheless, up to now, the majority of studies focus on the characteristics of textures and microstructures or how microtextures influence dwell fatigue life of titanium alloys [11–16]. There are few reports about the strength, ductility and Young's modulus with respect to the textures and microstructures from edge to center.
About the cross section of titanium alloys bar, its flow stress was more complex by comparing the longitudinal section during plastic deformation. Furthermore, the information obtained by studying cross section is less than section of axial direction. As a consequence, studies on microstructure and texture evolution of radial direction were very few [10,17]. Nonetheless, mechanical properties of materials in axial direction are related to microstructure and texture of radial direction. The service life of fan disk in aero-engine and rise string joint of drilling riser had a close relationship with radial mechanical properties [18]. Thus, it is very meaningful to study changes of microstructure and texture in radial direction and corresponding mechanical properties.
In present work, a big scale Ti6242s bar was selected for object of this study and the goals of this work are to: (1) investigate microstructures and textures evolution in center, mid-radius and edge and (2) clarify the relationship between microstructures, textures and mechanical properties of Ti6242s alloy.
2

Experimental and methods

The Ti6242S titanium alloy used in this investigation was provided by Northwest Institute of Nonferrous Metal of China. It was received in forms of a bar with an outer diameter of 260 mm and length of 129 mm respectively. The cast was produced from three vacuum arc smelting. Then it was cogged in the temperature range of 1150–1180 °C. After cogging, initial forging operations took place above β-transus to break up the coarse ingot structure. In order to refine the prior β phase grains and further obtain finer α phase lamellar by β→α transformation after cooling, the material was forged seven pass above the β-transus. After each forging pass, material was cooled to room temperature and was polished to remove the cracked parts. And then heating it up to above the βT temperature and forging again, the forging temperature of each pass decrease gradually. Second forging took place below β-transus within α+β phase field. Also, material was forged many times (five pass) so that uniformly equiaxed grains microstructure can be attained. The forging temperature was selected in range of below 30–50 °C of βT temperature, during which the rectangular billet was forged to a round billet with 270 mm diameter. Finally, a heat treatment was performed at low α+β field temperature to get a desirable microstructure. Then by a machine work, reducing the diameter to 260 mm. Precise forging parameters of the bar at intermediate stages of process is unknown for author because of the secrecy reasons. By ultrasonic tests, it is demonstrated that this product satisfied the industrial standard. Three experimental specimens were selected from edge, mid-radial and center position of this bar, as shown in Fig. 1, which were labeled as S1, S2 and S3 and the dimensions of sample was 10*10*5 mm. They were ground by using 150#,1000# and 2000# SiC abrasive paper and then were mechanical polished on a machine with a solution of water and Cr2O3 polishing agent until they become a mirror surface. Then they were etched by etching solution with proportion of (HF): (HNO3): (H2O) = 5:3:10. The microstructures were observed by Optical Microscopy and macrotextures were measured by Bruke D8 X-ray advanced diffraction detector with Cu target. The electric current and voltage were selected as 40 MA and 40 MV respectively. Macrotextures analysis were performed by TEXEval 2.5 soft. Specimens for XRD were same as optical microscopy, but polished again in order to eliminate the influence of contaminated layers if there is any. In order to further investigate feature of microstructures. After XRD tests, specimens were etched by ion about 70 min so as to eliminate residual stress on the surface of specimens. Then Electron Backscattered Diffraction (EBSD) technology was performed on S3400N Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) equipped with an EBSD detector. The step was set up by 0.3 μm which was relatively small so that high resolution could be obtained. Because the microstructure and texture of cross section corresponding to mechanical properties of axial direction. After these experiments, nine tensile samples derived from edge, R/2 and center position were prepared for tensile tests (three samples were taken at each position), which carried on equipment of INSTRON 1185 universal testing machine. In order to improve the accuracy of data, three times tensile tests were prepared for every position and relevant data were recorded by a program on the computer after tests. The average value of Tensile strength, Yield strength, Ductility and Young's modulus were calculated in terms of stress-strain curves.
3

Results and discussion

3.1

The variation of microstructures

The microstructures of S1, S2, S3 are shown in Fig. 2. It is obviously noticed that microstructures in three positions contain equiaxial grains and lath-like grains, which are named as the primary alpha (αp) phase and secondary alpha (αs) phase respectively, αs formed colony structures and separated by thin β layers. However, some differences can be seen among the three images, comparing with S2 and S3, both αp and αs grains in S1 is the smallest. The average size of αp grains are nearly equal in R/2 and center position. However, the size of the αs grains has an increasing trend from side to center. It is also noticed that the majority of αp grains have finished the process of globalization in three position. In addition, from IPF maps of S1, S2 and S3, it is observed that the features of αs laths disappear. In general, the αs laths belong to same colony have close orientation and the misorientation between them are less than 1°, so that similar color were given by HKL Channel 5 Soft [11]. From the scatter pole figure, it is noticed that no obvious preferred crystallographic orientation occurred.
The difference of plane stress distribution and cooling rate from edge to center is the most important factors to influence the development of microstructures of the titanium alloys stock. In general, for the longitudinal section, kinking or bent would be taken place if α lamellar grains which formed in the last forging process above β tarsus are parallel to the compressional axis. When α lamellar grains were perpendicular to the compressional axis, they will be easier to form equiaxial grains. If the α lamellar grains and compressional axis are at a certain angle between 0 and 90°, they could be rotated until perpendicular to the compressional axis [12]. However, for transverse section, it reflects the projection of microstructures of longitudinal section. Davies [10] observed that this section had a strong tendency to contain the minor axis of the αp grains. Hence, it seems that uniformly equiaxed αp grains appears in cross section with greater chance. The results also agree with the investigation of Z.B.Zhao [17]. Due to the plane stress in center is the most and the cooling rate is the slowest, it is more sufficient for the αp grains to make dynamic recovery(DRV) and dynamic recrystallization(DRX) occurrence, which transform the αp laths (formed in forging above β-transus) into equiaxial grains. Meanwhile, β grains in center ought to have larger volume and size due to the highest temperature caused by heat effect during the forging process and the longest heat preservation time. Thus, more larger αs colonies formed with wider αs size by β→α transition after cooling. For the edge location, the cooling rate is fairly quickly because of the dissipation of heat, which lead to the smallest αp and αs grains. Especially, it is full of difficulties for αs grains to see with naked eyes if the metallographs was magnified 200 times. The size of αp grains in R/2 position are nearly similar to center's whereas the average size of αs grains are about half of these grains in centers. As a matter of general experience, because of the longest hold time, the αp grains in center should continue to grow on the basis of mid-radial's grains, yet it is contradictory with results of this experiment. In fact, due to the prior β grains have larger size in center position, thus larger colonies of αs lath have been formed by β→α, which may restrain the further growth of αp grains.
By further careful observation, there are traces of globalization of prior αp laths, which require αp lamellar to make DRX or DRV occurrence and has to connect with deformation condition. Spontaneously, because of the different deformation state in different location, perhaps the spheroidization mechanism of prior αp phase laths from edge to center are different, following 3.4 section will discuss the issues in detail.
3.2

The variation of X-ray diffraction patterns

The X-ray diffraction patterns of S1, S2 and S3 are shown in Fig. 3. A good match for 2θ is observed by comparing PDF card data of α-Ti and β-Ti. It is noticed that the relative intensity of α (101¯2) and α (112¯0) plane increase gradually from S1 to S3, which indicate the two planes have a tendency of preferred orientation. Karthikeyan [19] investigated the effect of cooling rate on variant selection during phase transformation and he observed strong variant selection with (112¯0) ||ND under slower cooling rates. The conclusion is also reasonable to interpret this experimental phenomenon of the strongest α (112¯0) peak in S3 because the cooling rate of this position was slower than the location in S1 and S2. Meanwhile, the gradually increasing peak of α (112¯0) plane also indicated (111) planar texture of β phase since the process obeyed Burgers orientation relationship (BOR).
One can also observe that the strength of α (0002) peak relative to β (110) peak decrease gradually from S1 to S3, which indicated that more volume fraction of β phase have transformed into α phase during cooling in center. Hence, transformation texture is more likely to form in S3. The relative intensity of β (200) peak was also low in S1 and S2, but was higher in S3, thus the (200) plane of β phase have preferred crystallographic orientation in center although little β phase content remained at ambient temperature for Ti6242S alloy.
3.3

The evolution of mactotextures

In this work, the macrotextures are presented by (0002) and (112¯0) pole figure, as show in Fig. 4. According to the definition of pole figure and symmetry of the HCP crystal structure. For sample of S1, it contains three main textures which are marked with A, B and C respectively, the density of them are very weak. In particular, the texture component A with a maximum intensity of 1.52 times above random texture which located at the 70°from [0002] direction and about 30° from RD1 to RD2 direction. The texture component B deviate from ±10° around the RD2 and the maximum intensity is 1.36 times above random texture, the Miller index is (21¯1¯0)[033¯0]. The texture component C was represented by [0001] ~ 10° to RD1, the maximum intensity is 1.36 times above random texture. So the macrotexture is quietly spread and the intensity is very weak in side, it can be regarded as random textures.
For the specimen of S2, two main textures were formed and remarked by D and E respectively. The component of D locates at RD1 direction with maximum intensity of 2.89 times above random texture and its Miller index is (112¯0) [11¯00], a classical T-type texture in rolling plates of titanium alloys. The components of E with maximum intensity of 1.97 times above random texture and located in RD2 direction, the Miller index is (11¯00)[0001].
For the specimen of S3, Symmetrical cubic texture was formed, which can be related to α+β→α phase transformation [20,21]. Although identical in texture type to rolling texture above β-transus which has been reported by Lütjering [21], yet the center temperature did not exceed β-transus because of the existence of αp phase. It also can be divided into three texture components which are labeled as D, E and F respectively. Unlike the position in side and R/2, the maximum intensity is quite high in S3 with 7.91 and 3.99 times above random texture for components of D and E. The texture component F is situated on the 45°from RD1 to RD2 and away about 30°from [0001] direction. Its Miller index is (2¯114)[ 01¯10] and the maximum intensity is 1.82 times above random texture, which is identified as the transformation texture [22].
During the process of forging titanium alloys, plane strain deformation can be regard as the main deformation model for big scale bar. For transverse section, the section parallel to axis direction withstands pressure stress, at the same time, metal flows to radial direction. Therefore, it is similar to the process of rolling. The deformation temperature determines the texture type whereas deformation mode determines the symmetry of texture [10,20,21]. Owing to the larger stress on the location of R/2 and center comparing with edge during the forging process, grains are more likely to rotate and further develop into deformation texture. In general, plastic deformation occurs by activating slip and twin systems, due to the c/a of α-Ti phase is about 1.58, which is less than the ratio of idea HCP structure, so the prism <a> slip is activated preferentially in titanium alloys [17,22]. Hence, the texture component D and E were formed both in R/2 and center location. One can notice that the texture intensity of center is larger than R/2 which suggests larger deformation plane stress in center. Meanwhile, the transformation texture component F appeared because the sufficient β→α transformation and the occurrence of variants selection, which coincided with regular of XRD diffraction patterns (as is seen in Fig. 3). For edge, the rapid cooling rate and unrestrained flowing stress dispersed and softened the texture features, thus the intensity is not as large as expected.
3.4

The spheroidization mechanism of αp grains from edge to center

There are generally two ways for αp laths to make spheroidization during forging at α+β field temperature, which are grain boundary migration mechanism and deformation-induced mechanism respectively. In the first case, DRX is required, whereas in the second they are formed by stress induced and only perhaps DRV happened [10,12]. In general, the higher strain storage energy, the better DRX. Recrystallization and recovery are accomplished by consuming low angle grain boundaries(LABs). As a result, the grain boundaries of recrystallization grain are high angle grain boundaries (HABs), however, subgrains can be formed by DRV and the grain boundaries concentrate in the range of 5–10°. Fig. 5 shows the grain boundary maps and attaching the misorientation distribution diagram in the upper right. It is noticed that the most of the misorientation between adjacent grains are concentrated within 10° range in edge. Consequently, the process of spheroidization was completed by deformation-induced mechanism. Nevertheless, the proportion of low angle boundaries is decreased in R/2 and center position, conversely, the high angle boundaries account for a significant proportion of 43.2% and 63.7% respectively. Thus, the equiaxed grains were formed by grain boundary migration mechanism in R/2 and center positions. Owing to the higher strain energy state, the degree of DRX in center position took place more adequately by comparing R/2 position.
3.5

The activation of slip and twin system

According to Von Mises yield criterion, five slip systems are required for plastic deformation, but prism <a> slip have only three slip systems. Hence, the other slip systems may be activated to participate plastic deformation because the deformation of materials cannot be completed only by prism <a> slip systems. However, because of the nonuniformity of strain among grains, it is possible to plastic deformation with less than five slip systems [12]. Especially, the development of microstructures and formation of misorientation were linked with movements of slip and twins systems [12,23,24]. During the process of plastic deformation, the axis of lattice rotation (Wp) could be determined by activating slip plane(n→) and the direction (a→) of slip systems, which can be expressed by Ref. [12]:
Wp=n→×a→
It is no doubt that the formation of misorientation was associated with rotation of neighboring grains. Taking this for further consideration, among the possible slip systems of α titanium. Prism <a> slip systems rotate adjacent grains in opposite direction and further make misorientation raise during the process of plastic deformation [12,23]. The activation of slip systems is mainly dependent on the value of Critical Resolved Shear Stress(CRSS), and moreover, the CRSS are mainly effected by yield stress and deformation temperature. Higher temperature corresponds to lower CRSS, so in R/2 and center position, thanks to higher temperatures and longer soaking times, the CRSS are relatively smaller, the prism <a> slip systems are easier to start. Thus, the HABs was formed in both position. However, in edge, the plane stress and temperature is the lowest because of the rapid cooling rate. So LABs appeared in side. Moreover, the quantitative relationship between misorientation and slip systems need to be further investigated.
Fig. 6 and Fig. 7 show Schmid Factors (SF) distribution diagram of Prism <a> system and Twin boundary maps of sample S1, S2 and S3 respectively. It is observed that hardly any twins appear in all position which could be inhibited by AL elements [20], but the distribution of SF was concentrated upon scope of 0.42–0.48. By contrast, center have the highest frequency distribution in this range. Hence, the prism <a> slip systems is the responsible for evolution of microstructures and textures. The investigation of JIN Yun-xue suggested that normal direction of {0001} basal plane deviates towards TD when Prism<a> slip dominant deformation during rolling process(the TD corresponding to XO direction in the work) [25]. So texture component D and E was formed with high intensity since the prism<a> slips are more likely to activate in center position. By comparison, in R/2 position, the intensity of texture is lower due to the relatively lower temperature and plane stress than center position.
3.6

The variation of mechanical properties in different position

The stress-strain curves in edge, R/2 and center position attained by tensile tests are show in Fig. 8. The calculated corresponding value of mechanical properties are listed in Table 1. It can be summarized that hardly all mechanical properties are the best in edge but worst in the center (In addition to ductility, which had little changes from side to center). Besides, the tensile and yield strength drop dramatically in center location by comparing other two locations. It is also observed that center has higher Young's modulus than R/2.

The mechanical properties of metallic materials changes depend on the variation of microstructures and textures. For titanium alloys, microtextures can reduce the dwell fatigue performance dramatically, while the macrotextures will lead to anisotropy such as Strength, Plasticity and Young's modulus [13–16,26,27]. For edge, the size of both αp and αs grains are very fine so that a large amount of grain boundaries formed among them, through the grain boundary strengthening (one of the main strengthening mechanisms in metal materias), the strength and plasticity improved simultaneously. In addition, the intensity of macrotextures in edge is nearly random, which means more homogenous mechanical properties have been attained. For the R/2 position, the microstructure is mainly composed of equiaxial αp grains with few αs lath colonies. Meanwhile, the main texture components of D and E represent (112¯0) and (11¯00)//RD1-RD2 plane respectively and they have moderate intensity. The basal plane is the close-packed plane of atoms of HCP structure whose strength and hardness are the highest when the loading perpendicular to it but good plasticity can be got if loading parallel to it. Hence, good match obtained between strength and plasticity in this position (Materials with equiaxed grains have good match of strength and plasticity). For center position, the volume fraction of αs laths colonies is larger than R/2 position but opposite of equiaxed αp grains. The larger αs laths colonies and size are responsible for the decrease of tensile and yield strength [2,21]. Moreover, the main macrotextures components D and E have the maximum intensity of 7.91 and 3.99 times above random texture, which are very strong texture within materials. They can also be contributed to the further reduction of tensile strength. As a consequence, more number of larger colonies combine strong texture jointly lead to the tensile strength and yield strength drop dramatically in center position.
As regards the Young's modulus, which reflects the difficulty level at which atoms deviate from its equilibrium position and it is not susceptible to microstructures, the solid solution phase and textures are two main factors to influence Young's modulus [2]. In this work, the main texture components are D and E, and their spatial orientation can be represented by Fig. 9. For crystalline materials, the close-packed plane has the highest Young's modulus but the least densely packed crystal plane has the lowest Young's modulus. Therefore, the Young' s modulus of basal plane is significant higher than prismatic planes for HCP structure [28]. As this in mind, according to above discussion, it is reasonable for edge to have the highest value of Young's modulus because of its nearly random texture, the Young' s of center position should be lowest due to the largest maximal intensity of texture components of D and E by comparing the R/2 position. However, in fact, a reverse measuring result was observed, the texture component F may be responsible for this. Bache [28] reported that the Young's modulus of α titanium as a function of declination angle γ between c-aixs of the unit cell and stress axis. The modulus varies between 145Gpa (stress axis parallel to the c-axis) and 100Gpa (stress axis perpendicular to the c-axis). When the F texture appears, it means that the (2¯114) plane lie in RD1-RD2 plane, and the c-axis of the unit cell are at about 30°angle with RD1-RD2 plane, Young's modulus of single crystal in this direction is about 1.3 times about the condition of D and E texture (tensile axis perpendicular to the c-axis) for a single crystal. Therefore, it can be deduced that the appearance of F texture can increase the Young's modulus of materials significantly. Beyond that, the slowest cooling rate boost more sufficient β→α transformation. As a result, more amount of α stabilizing elements were concentrated in the α phase, which also increase the Young's modulus [18].
4

Conclusion

The microstructures, textures and corresponding mechanical properties in radial direction of a near α titanium alloy bar from side to center was investigated. Following conclusions can be obtained:
  • (1)
    In edge, smaller plane stress and rapid cooling rate leaded fine αp and αs grains to be formed, plus a nearly random macrotexture. The most excellent mechanical properties were obtained.
  • (2)
    In R/2, most of grains are equixial αp and (112¯0) [11¯00] and (112¯0) [0001] texture component come into being, which result in good match between tensile strength and ductility.
  • (3)
    In center, the width of αs grains is about two times of R/2 and with larger αs colonies, the size of αp is almost similar to R/2 but smaller volume fraction. Besides, strong symmetrical cubic texture was formed, all of these factors led to tensile strength and yield drop quickly in center position.
  • (4)
    Young's modulus was mainly influenced by macrotextures, the texture of edge was random, so it has the largest Young's modulus. (112¯0) [11¯00] and (112¯0) [0001] texture components were developed in both R/2 and center position, but the maximum intensity of texture was larger in center. So Young's modulus of center should be smaller than R/2. But a reverse result was observed, the (2¯114)[011¯0] texture and more amount of α stabilizing elements were concentrate in the α phase of center were responsible for it.
  • (5)
    The spheroidization of αp lamellars were accomplished by strain-induced in edge but DRX in R/2 and center position.
  • (6)
    Further EBSD analysis indicated that the prism <a> slip system was responsible for microstructure and texture evolution.
