Microscopic, elemental and molecular spectroscopic investigations of root-knot nematode infested okra plant roots

Abstract

Okra (Abelmoschus esculentus (L.) Moenche) is one of the warm season crops that is grown in the tropical and subtropical regions of the world. Root-knot nematodes, Meloidogyne spp. is major constraints in growing okra crops and reduce the crop yield. The present study was aimed to perform microscopic, elemental and molecular spectroscopic investigations of root-knot nematode infested okra plant roots. The healthy, root-knot infested and root-knot infested with fungus okra roots have been studied to study the changes in chemical composition caused by infection with root-knot nematodes. We have utilized binocular spectroscopy for microscopic observations of nematode species in infected okra plant roots. Wavelength dispersive X-ray fluorescence spectrometry has been used to detect and quantify the minerals and heavy metals in healthy, nematode infested and nematode infested with fungus okra roots. Numerous major, minor and heavy elements were quantified which have shown their toxic effects in for developing root-knot nematodes in okra plant roots. We have observed the changes in the concentrations of major and heavy elements in nematode infected okra root samples as compared to healthy root samples and in particular higher contents of Ca, K, S, Ti, Sr, and Ni is detected in nematode infected okra roots than that from healthy root samples. Changes in contents of heavy metals Al and Cr were also observed in infected roots. No significant changes have been observed in the concentrations of heavy elements Br, Ni, Rb, and Zr in healthy as well as nematode infested roots. Ag and Pd have only been detected in nematode infested okra roots. Fourier transforms infrared and ultravioletvisible spectroscopy is employed to investigate the molecular changes in the nematode infested roots as compared to healthy roots. Fourier transform infra-red spectral studies revealed the decreasing content of starch and increased level of protein contents in root-knot infested roots as compared with healthy roots. Intensity of absorption peaks appeared in diffused reflectance spectra was observed lower for infected roots and higher in healthy roots. The present study is important for further treatment strategies of okra crop to prevent the crop loss in effective manner. The authors have utilized different techniques like wavelength dispersive X-ray fluorescence; fourier transforms infrared and ultravioletvisible spectroscopy along with optical microscopy in plant science to study healthy and infected plant samples.

Highlights

  • •
    Mineral elements and heavy metal elements associated with nematode and fungal infections have been successfully identified and quantified in okra roots.
  • •
    FTIR spectroscopy has been successfully used to studies the changes in starch, carbohydrates, protein, fatty acids, lipids, contents in infected okra roots. Significant changes in the intensities of absorption peaks in healthy and infected okra roots have also been observed and explained.
  • •
    We demonstrated the utility of the spectroscopic methods such as FTIR, UVVis and XRF for its use in plant science to explain the role of the uptake of heavy metals and to determine the role of complex molecules in diseased plants.

Introduction

Root-knot nematodes have been observed as one of the threats for global food production [1]. Due to the infestation in the tropical agriculture, the crop loss was estimated to be 5–43% [2] which depends upon the nematode species, crops and geographical regions [3,4]. In India, the annual estimated crop losses have been figured out to be nearly Rs. 242.1 billion due to major plant parasitic nematodes [5]. Most of the crop plants that accounts for majority of world's food supply and weeds are greatly influenced by root knot nematodes. However [6], reported that M. incognita and M. javanica have the widest host range infecting more than 232 and 114 genera of plants respectively. In particular, aromatic plants, pulses, fibre crops, fruits, vegetables, ornamentals, medicinal other important crops have been found affected greatly by root knot nematodes. Therefore, the information on yield loss in various crops is lacking and often qualitative. 70% loss has been reported in chilli, brinjal, tomato and okra by Ref. [7] [8]. reported that 82.5% plants of tomato, brinjal and okra were infected with M. incognita and M. javanica [9]. outlined losses in yields of okra (90.9%), tomato (46.2%) and brinjal (27.3%) due to the infestation with M. incognita.
Okra (Abelmoschus esculentus (L.) Moench) is one of the most economically important agricultural crops cultivated throughout the tropical and warm temperate regions of the world [10]. Commercially, it is grown in several countries including India. It has been found very rich in protein, moisture, ash, lipids, carbohydrates, fat [11] [12]; and in minerals as well such as calcium (Ca), iron (Fe), magnesium (Mg), manganese (Mn), nickel (Ni), potassium (K), sodium (Na), and zinc (Zn), [13]. Due to the presence of high quality of edible oil and high level of proteins, it is consumed as one of the protein sources [14].
Okra has also been found to be highly prone to root-knot nematodes and then the root-knot infested plants are stunted and exhibits symptoms of nutritional deficiencies, characteristics huge swellings on primary as well as secondary root systems [15,16]. Various Meloidogyne species attack maximum crops and greatly affects not only yields but also its quality [3]. Root-knot nematodes, Meloidogyne spp. are pondered as one of the most crucial pest problems affecting broad varieties of important agricultural crops globally. This disease is the prime constraints in growing okra successfully throughout the tropical and sub tropical regions. The yield loss, however, may be as high as 60% in continuously cultivated crop of vegetables due to root-knot nematode [17]. The role of root-knot nematode and their management strategies in okra crop has not been investigated particularly in Jammu region. Hence, a study was undertaken to assess the damage caused by M. incognita on okra.
Due to increase in continuous cultivation, nowadays all species of plants are subjected to diseases from several kinds of pathogens (like nematodes, fungi, and bacteria etc.), environmental conditions, and deficiency of minerals. Okra has been considered to be highly prone to gall nematodes and plants that are associated with this type of nematode have large galls or swelling on every part of the roots. These nematodes inject their hormones into the plant's roots and damage it by reducing its capability to absorb water and mineral nutrients and hence, interfering with photosynthates and the translocation of minerals inside the plants [18].
The sign and symptoms of root-knot nematodes are relatively undistinguished, including deficiency of mineral nutrients, stunting, incipient wilt, poor yield and most of the time plant death. Some diagnostic symptoms of root-knot affected plants exists except root gall formation, cysts, seed galls which cannot be sensed earlier and this leads to huge damage to crops which often goes unnoticed. Damage due to nematodes starts from a small area and spreads radially from the initial infection site to a large farm area. Even a lot of work has been done on the control measurements against the root-knot nematode on different vegetable crops but still the suitable measure to control nematode could not witnessed yet. Many farmers are using pesticides in order to control these diseases which are not effective and useful as their mode of action is not specific to one species. Thus, the only way to accurately diagnose nematode diseases is to study soil and plant samples from some suspected sites.
In past few years, the applications of elemental and molecular spectroscopic techniques have focused mainly on the studies of plant samples in order to get their elemental information and structural changes due to infections caused by several diseases. The species identification process involves various biological, biochemicals, morphological, molecular features, to identify the complex compounds present in the infected plants species. Advances in spectroscopic techniques such as fourier-transform infra-red (FTIR), ultra-violet visible (UVVis), and wavelength dispersive X-ray fluorescence (WD-XRF) spectroscopy have tackled many problems quickly associated with plants and offers the chemical and structural information and changes due to diseased infections without any specific sample preparation method. Spectroscopic technique such as FTIR has been applied to get the structural and molecular information of okra species [19]. Similarly, molecular technique such as UVVis spectroscopy has also been employed to identify the polyphenolic complex compounds from okra seeds [20]. Recently, Singh et al. [21], have successfully quantified the heavy and toxic elements in wheat seed gall nematodes and also differentiated the infected wheat grains affected by Anguina trirtci with the healthy wheat grains on the basis of the spectral changes observed employing WDXRF, FTIR, and diffuse reflectance spectroscopy. Spectroscopic studies on nematode affected okra plants have not been extensively studied to investigate the heavy and toxic metal accumulation and changes in the molecular contents. The role of root-knot nematode and their management strategies in okra crop is very much crucial. So, the present spectroscopic investigations on nematode infected okra plants were carried out at various localities of Jammu division.
Therefore, our aim was to analyze the nematode infected okra plant using spectroscopic techniques FTIR, UVVis, WD-XRF, and LIBS and also to compare the data with uninfected Okra plants to investigate the changes for its therapeutic strategies. Elemental profile of the okra plant samples has been achieved using LIBS and WD-XRF in order to quantify the mineral contents in okra and also to observe the changes due to the formation of galls. UVVis data of uninfected and infected okra plant samples was useful to investigate the spectral differences among the healthy and diseased plants. FTIR spectroscopy has been employed to investigate the chemical composition of the root-knot infested and healthy okra plant sample. The present study was very much important as literature is silent with regards of the spectroscopic investigations of the nematode infected okra plants.

Material and methods

2.1

Sample collection

Okra plant roots (uninfected, infected by root-knot nematodes, and infected by root-knot nematodes associated with Rhizoctonia solani fungus) have been collected from the highly suspected field site in Jammu from Jammu region. The infected plants were procured from the farmers fields. The collected plant samples first washed to remove the extra dirt and soil and then dried. The visual study was done to investigate the presence of root galls which can be seen from Fig. 1. Fig. 1a shows the snapshot of healthy/uninfected okra plant roots. From, Fig. 1b, one can see the clear symptoms of gall formation in roots which shows that the okra plant was infected by root-knot nematodes. Fig. 1c shows the snapshot of the root-knot nematode associated with Rhizoctonia solani fungus which was further identified experimentally. After drying, plant root samples were grounded using mortar and pestle in the form of fine powder. Before proceeding further characterization, the infected okra plant samples were observed and examined carefully to show root-gall nematode then further characterized using advanced spectroscopic techniques.
2.2

Characterization techniques

As accurate identification of nematodes to specific species is very crucial for their control and is a prerequisite to meaningful research. Many nematodes species are easily identified due to the appearing of large round shape galls or swells on the roots or other parts of the plant and some are difficult to identify because of their poor taxonomical description. Therefore, optical microscopy has also been used for the detection of the presence of the nematodes in the okra plant.
2.3

Characterization

The grounded okra plant samples in fine powder were characterized using FTIR, UVVis, and WD-XRF spectroscopy techniques. The FTIR spectra of Okra root samples (healthy as well as infected) were captured using a RZX (Perkin Elmer) spectrometer in the spectral range 4000–400 cm−1 with 1 cm−1 of spectral resolution. The spectrum were captured in transmittance mode and converted in absorbance mode followed by base line subtraction and normalization. The UVVis spectra (in the range 190–1400 nm) of uninfected and infected okra plant samples have been captured employing Shimadzu UV-2600 double beam spectrophotometer. Nematode species were examined and identified using optical microscope (Leica Microsystems) used in transmittance mode.
Elemental analysis of okra plant has been carried out using WD-XRF spectrometry. WD-XRF spectra has been recorded by using S8 TIGER WDXRF spectrometer equipped with a 4 KWatt Rh anode X-ray tube (60 kV max; and 170 mA max) as a energy source which was equipped with a proportional flow counter and a scintillation counter. Quantification of trace minerals and heavy and toxic elements were carried out using a standard less software “Quant Express” which was provided along with the instruments. The measured and maximum relative standard deviation (RSD) was found nearly up to maximum of 5%.

Results and discussion

3.1

Identification of nematodes and fungus

Root-knot nematodes, Meloidogyne spp. were observed the most significant pest problems in several villages of Jammu from Jammu and Kashmir state of India. Sever plant-stunting, chlorosis, and immense root galling were detected on okra at farmers field. Investigations of the infected okra root samples revealed the presence of abundant root-knot nematodes. Using morphological characteristics females, males, and juveniles were extracted by cobb's sieving and decanting method for further species identification. The specific identification of the root-knot nematode was examined by cutting perineal pattern of the females. For this study, the procedure adopted was described by Ref. [22]. As described by Goodey et al. [22], firstly the galled roots separated from the affected okra roots were cleaned in water and immersed in a beaker which contains boiling 0.1% cotton blue and for clearing it was left for whole night. The female nematodes were teased out from the galls and transferred to a drop of lactophenol which was taken on a glass slide. The posterior portion of the females was cut very carefully employing a clean and sharp razor blade and body contents were cleaned. The perineal pattern of adult females was trimmed and mounted for investigation. For each investigation, ten slides were prepared containing the perineal pattern, head shape, stylet length, and length of the juveniles of nematode. Species identification was carried out by comparing the observation characteristics in the perineal region using methods described by Eisenback et al., [23]. Thus, our investigations revealed that the root-knot nematode species which affected the okra roots was identified as Meloidogyne incognita.
Also, the root-knot nematode associated with fungus on okra was isolated on potato dextrose agar (PDA) medium and identified as Rhizoctonia solani. The identified species of nematode and the fungus associated with the infected okra plant are shown in fig. (1) in which (a) represents M. incognita in nematode infected okra plant roots and (b) represents association of R. Solani fungus with M. incognita in nematode association with fungus okra plant roots. The pathogenicity was tested of both pathogens like M. incognita and R. solani and without pathogens as control on okra (cv. Pusa Sawani) plant in pot culture with sterilized sandy loam soil. Dissociation of roots and their microscopic examination confirmed infection of root-knot nematodes.
3.2

Detection of root-knot nematodes of infected okra by optical microscopy

To cross verify the above results, we have also used optical microscopy method for pointing out the presence of nematodes in the infected okra plant. In the present work, for staining of nematodes of okra roots, we have followed the methods described by Ref. [24]. Firstly, the infested roots were crushed employing a mortar and pestle arrangement and then root samples were immersed in a boiling solution of lactophenol cotton blue for 5 min and left for 30 min in the cooling dye solution. To separate excess amount of stains, the okra plant root were cleaned in running tap water several times and mounted in the solution lactophenol. Employing an optical microscope operated in transmittance mode (Decker, 1989), the blue stained nematodes were clearly examined (Fig. 2). Thus, the microscopic observations described above clearly indicated the presence of nematodes inside the roots of okra plant root.
3.3

FTIR spectroscopy

An FTIR spectrum provides the information's about the functional groups of the organic constituents present in the roots of okra plant. The normalized FTIR spectra of okra root (uninfected, nematodes infected and nematode infected associated with R. Solani fungus) samples were recorded and are given in Figs. 3 and 4. In Table 1, we have summarized all the FTIR peaks with their corresponding peak assignments with bibliography. The FTIR spectra of healthy and nematode infected okra roots were recorded in transmittance mode and then it was converted into absorbance. After subtracting the base line, the spectrums were normalized to compare the molecular contents presents in the healthy and infected roots. As can be seen (Figs. 3 and 4), the relative intensities of the absorption peaks observed in FTIR spectra of healthy and diseased samples are different and hence indicating the relative changes in the chemical constituents of okra roots due to nematodes and virus. Typical FTIR spectra of okra roots were captured to examine the protein, fat, carbohydrates, starch content and moisture contents. The spectra were also compared to study the relevant changes in healthy and infected root samples which indicated the relative change in the contents of proteins, and starch etc. Spectra of healthy and root-knot infested okra roots contains absorption peaks due to OH and NH stretching, protein (amide 1 and amide 2) absorption bands, CH stretching bands, starch characteristics bands although the relative intensities and their patterns are quite dissimilar. Typically, the absorption bands and their intensities identified in the FTIR spectra represent the molecular constituents and their relative contents of the okra plant roots.

FTIR is the most evasive method to measure the presence of moisture content in the plants. The moisture of the healthy and infected roots was measured using absorption bands appeared in the mid-IR range. Appearance of absorption peak at 3380 cm−1 is due to OH stretching and hence is the indicative of water content [34]. Water molecule absorbs strongly in the IR spectral region due to the H bending vibrations and OH stretching, however its quantification is sometimes obstructed by other compounds containing OH including alcohols, phenols and hydro peroxides [39].
The existence of protein content in okra roots is designated by the appearance of absorption peaks at 1657 cm−1 and 1542 cm−1 which is due to the amide 1 and amide 2 respectively. The absorption peaks observed. Absorption peak due to amide 1 in the range 1600–1700 cm−1 arises due to CO stretching of the peptide group of protein molecules of okra roots. The amide 2 band in the range 1510–1580 cm−1 arises basically from the combination of NH bending vibrations and CN stretching vibrations which is the amide backbone of proteins. Thus, protein content was measured due to the peaks of the amide 1bands in the range 1600–1700 cm−1 and amide 2 in the range 1550–1570 cm−1 [25,27,29,30] [26]; [27,33,34]. The peak at 1516 cm−1 of okra roots is attributed to the combinations of NH bending and CN stretching in the amide 2 band of protein molecules [37]. The infected okra root sample (sample 2 and 3) is rich in protein than that from healthy okra root samples, as exhibited by the sizeable amide1 and amide 2 FTIR bands. The increasing intensities of absorption peaks at 1657 and 1542 cm−1 (Figs. 3 and 4) in infected okra roots clearly indicated that the protein constituent is greatly affected by the nematode infection by M. incognita and fungal infections by R. Solani in okra roots and hence showing increase in the protein components.
One of the major constituents of the okra is starch and carbohydrates. The absorption bands of starch in their FTIR spectra are typically from the vibrational modes of its main components particularly amylose and amylopectin. In our work, the corresponding FTIR absorption peaks were noticed in the spectral regions below 800 cm−1, from 800 to 1500 cm−1 which is the fingerprint region, from 2800 to 3000 cm−1 (region of CH stretching), and from 3000 to 3600 cm−1 (region of OH stretching). The IR spectra of mono- and oligosaccharides are much valuable for small molecular identification and also in polymeric analysis. For analysis of carbohydrates, the most frequently used IR spectral region is the anomeric region 950-750 cm−1. In this spectral region, it is generally feasible to discriminate the characteristic bands of ‘a’ and ‘b’ conformers, or pyranoid and furanoid ring vibrations of monosaccharide and polysaccharide. Furthermore, the absorbance bands that are typical characteristics of lipids, mainly, the CH stretching in the region 2800–3000 cm−1 [40] and absorption bands appeared at 1739 cm−1 is associated with the stretching vibration of (CO) of ester group mainly from lipids and fatty acids [32,41]. In our case, the absorption peak noticed at 2925, 2854, and 1739 cm−1 are ascribable to the ester linkage in okra roots. Another absorption peak appeared at 1454 cm−1 is mainly caused by methyl and methylene groups from lipids and proteins [32]. FTIR spectrum (Figs. 3 and 4) clearly indicated that the relative changes in absorption peak intensities in the fingerprint region particularly at (1023, 1052, 1081, 1104 and 1154 cm−1) are the indicative of starch, carbohydrates and glycogens contents in the uninfected okra root sample. The starch and other carbohydrates contents in healthy okra roots is greater as compared to infected samples which reveals that starch and other carbohydrates of okra roots is greatly affected by nematodes and fungal infections and that is accountable for the decreased starch level in infected roots [21,38].
From Fig. 4, it is clear that the absorbance intensities of starch bands is more in healthy okra roots than that of nematode infected and nematode infected associated with fungus okra roots. This clearly indicates that starch level is decreased in infected roots. Decreased starch level in diseased roots may be attributed to the increased amylase activity which is one of the components of cell starch. And thus, due to the increased amylase activity, it was hydrolyzed into easily assimilable simple sugar, such as maltose resulting into a lower concentration of starch. Tayal and Agrawal [42] also observed the increased activity of amylase in M. incognita infected Solanum melongena. The decreasing contents of carbohydrates was also observed and documented by Farahat et al. [43] in the roots of eggplant, papaya, jasmine infested with M. incognita nematodes which is in good agreement with our findings in case of okra roots.
From, Fig. 4, it can also be seen that the absorbance intensity of protein (amide 1 and amide 2) was found less in healthy okra roots than that from diseased roots which clearly indicate the presence of more protein contents in nematode infected roots as compared with healthy okra roots. More protein contents in infected roots is due to the stimulation of protein synthesis in infected roots and the amino acid were not simply released during hydrolysis of existing proteins [44].
Figs. 3 and 4 also revealed that the appreciable change in the absorbance intensity of the peak observed at 1739 cm−1 which arises due to the (CO) of ester group primarily from lipids and fatty acids. Thus, it may be concluded that nematode also affects the lipid and fatty acid contents of the okra plant roots. The absorption peak appeared at 3066 cm−1 (Fig. 3) appears due to the CH stretching vibration in cis double-bond = CH primarily from unsaturated fatty acids which is affected by the root knot nematodes and fungus (R. Solani).
FTIR spectroscopic method has been extensively applied to examine the chemical groups and the functional properties of infected and healthy okra roots. Here, in our work, we have successfully demonstrated that starch, proteins, and fats were the most important components of root samples which were greatly affected by the formation of galls in okra roots (as can be clearly seen in Fig. 1a, b, & 1c). Thus, it is concluded that formation of gall in roots unfavorably affects the chemical compositions of okra plants and responsible for crop destruction.
In the present work, FTIR spectra of uninfected, nematode infested and nematode association with fungus okra plant has been shown successfully to study the chemistry of the okra and the changes that occurred due to the interruption of nematode. The methyl, carbonyl and hydroxyl functional groups are the chemical constituents of the carbohydrate molecules present in the okra root and that is the main backbone of the plants. From the results, it has been cleared that the spectral changes that occur in the FTIR spectra must be due to the galls (knots) formation on the okra samples that affects its quality and also degraded its major constituents like protein, carbohydrates and fats. The results so obtained are very well matched with the work done so far in literature.
3.4

Diffuse reflectance study of root samples

UVVis spectra in diffuse reflectance mode of okra roots (uninfected, nematode infested and nematode infested associated with R. Solani fungus) have been recorded using Shimadzu UV-2600 Double beam spectrophotometer in wavelength region 190–1200 nm which is shown in Fig. 5. The root samples were scanned several times properly to evaluate its chemical compositions. The corresponding changes in the characteristics peaks have been observed for nematode infested and nematodes associated with fungus. The absorbance peaks were observed at 200 nm, 280 nm and 329 nm in the UV range only (Fig. 5). The absorption peak observed at 200 nm can be assigned to the phenolic compounds of the okra plant roots [21]. Iwu et al. [45] reported the rich presence of phenolic compounds in the seeds of okra which is mainly composed of oligomeric catechins and flavonol derivatives. Flavonoids are one kind of pigments of plants and are accountable for the coloration and discoloration of plant leaves, fruits, and flowers [46]. In our study, the peak appeared at 280 nm can be attributed to the presence of flavonoid. Catechins, a natural phenol and anti-oxidant are a second class of flavonoids has characteristics UVVis spectra, with maximum absorbance which is clearly quantified at 280 nm [46]. The intensity of absorption peaks was found higher in healthy roots than that from diseased okra roots. The concentration of phenolic compounds and flavonoids which are main components of okra plant are going to decrease as we move from healthy to high-infection okra samples due to nematode attack and can be clearly seen in the UVVis spectra. It may happen due to the degradation of the quality of the okra plant that takes place because of gall formation in the roots of okra plant. It is stated that flavonoids along with phenolic composition helps in resisting oxidative stress and act as growth regulators in the plant systems [47]. In our work, the quality of the okra plants has been influenced by the attack of nematode species and fungus. Flavonols exhibited two absorption peaks, one maximum at 273 nm which has been appeared at 280 nm with a shoulder peak at 329 nm. Absorption peak at 329 nm in fungal infected okra root due to R. Solani may be associated to the fungal infections. The degradation of phenolic compounds may be one of the responsible factors for the growth of plant pathogens such as nematodes and fungus of okra roots [48,49].

WD-XRF spectroscopic studies of okra roots

The recorded spectra have been plotted in the different energy range (Fig. 5) and the spectra have been studied thoroughly to monitor the presence of mineral nutrients, heavy metal elements and their changing patterns in diseased root samples. In Table 2, we have summarized the X-ray energies (particularly Kα1andKβ1) of all elements detected in the WDXRF spectra of okra plant root samples (1, 2, & 3). After that WD-XRF plot has been used to measure the quantity of heavy and trace metals of healthy and infected samples of okra plants using “Qunat Express” software that is already provided with the XRF spectrometer (S8-Berker). The elements detected in root samples of okra along with their concentrations have been tabulated in Table 3. Numerous elements were detected elements in root samples that are aluminium (Al), calcium (Ca), chlorine (Cl), copper (Cu), chromium (Cr), iron (Fe), magnesium (Mg), manganese (Mn), nickel (Ni), potassium (K), phosphorus (P), rubidium (Rb), silicon (Si), sodium (Na), sulphur (S), strontium (Sr), titanium (Ti), zinc (Zn) and zirconium (Zr). As can be seen from Table 3 that the concentration of observed elements in root samples are different in uninfected and infected root samples. The concentrations of the existing metal elements of the healthy and infected roots of okra and their correlation has been shown in the bar diagrams in Fig. 6a and b (see Fig. 7).

Analyzing WDEXRF data, we have observed that the concentration of minerals particularly, Ca, Cl, K, Mg, Na, and S are higher in nematode infected okra plant roots samples than that from healthy root samples. On the other hand mineral elements particularly Cu, Mn, P, Si, and Zn are found in lower amount in nematode infested root sample as compared to healthy okra roots. Al and Cr were also observed in lower amount in nematode infested root sample than that from healthy okra root. No change have been observed in the concentrations of elements Br, Ni, Rb, and Zr in healthy and nematode infested root samples. Silver (Ag) has been detected only in nematode infested okra roots and Pd has been detected only in nematode infested roots (nematode infected, nematode infected and associated with Rhizoctonia solani fungus).
The lower contents of elements Fe, P, and Zn in nematode infested okra roots (infected with M. incognita) as compared with healthy roots are in good accordance with the work reported by Farahat et al. [43]. Farahat et al. [43] observed the lower contents of Fe, P, and Zn in nematode infected (infected with M. incognita) root samples of cowpea, eggplant, citrus, jasmine and papaya as compared with healthy subjects. The decrease of phosphorous (P) content in nematode infested okra root sample as compared with healthy roots also is also supported by the report published by Sharma et al. [50]. Sharma et al. [50] performed quantitative estimation of various metabolites and mineral elements in healthy and M. incognita infected roots of highly susceptible (Pusa Swami) and susceptible (Punjab-7) cultivars of okra. They observed the decreased content of P in infected okra roots of Pusa Swami and Punjab-7 cultivars as compared with healthy roots. The authors also observed the increased content of Na in nematode infested root samples as compared to healthy roots. In the present investigation, we have also observed the increased contents of Na in nematode infested root samples as compared to healthy okra roots that are well endorsed by the documented reports by Sharma et al. [50].
Detection of Ag and Pd (non essential elements in plants) only in infected okra roots indicates their role in the formation of nematodes in okra. The contents of Sr and Ti in okra roots are found in increasing order in healthy, nematode infected, nematode infected and association with fungus roots respectively. Overall, the concentration of elements Ca, K, S, Ti, Zr, Ni and Sr are found in the increasing order as sample 1 > sample 2 > sample 3. In our investigation, we have also observed that the contents of some elements are found in decreased in nematode infected roots (samples 1) and then found increased in nematode infected and associated with R. Solani fungus as well (sample 3). This pattern can be seen in Table 3 and plotted as bar diagram (Fig. 6a and b).
The heavy elements particularly Al, Cr, and Ti were detected in very high amount in nematode and fungus infected okra roots (sample 3) as compared with nematode infected (sample 2) and healthy roots (sample 1) clearly indicated their role in nematode sand R. Solani fungal infections in okra roots. Higher concentration of aluminium (Al) can be one of the life-and-death threats to agricultural crops due to its inhibition of the growth the roots, inducing oxidative stress, induction of cellulose and peroxidation of cytoplasmic membrane, nutritional variance and death of plant cells [51]. These changes may appear due to the effect of the gall formation on the okra roots by the nematodes and fungal attack. The biological role of chromium (Cr) is not clear particularly in physio-logical and biochemical metabolism of plants [52]. Excessive Cr level can promote various morphological, physiological, and biochemical toxic effects in plants [53]. However, Cr toxicity in plants is well documented towards growth inhibition of plants and causes ultrastructural moderations of cell membrane and chloroplast, cell death in roots, diminish pigment contents, influence chlorosis in leaves, disturbing the relation of water flow and mineral nutrition, and change enzymatic activities [52].
From this study, we can concluded that the appearance of few heavy and noxious elements like Cr, Pd, Ag and Al are the changes that may appear with the action of nematode and R. Solani fungus. These toxic elements effect the growth, photosynthesis, nutrient uptake capacity of the plant and thereby reducing the quality of the okra. Thus, the changes in the elemental composition that appears can be considered as one of the results that happened due to nematode disease and the formation of galls.
4

Conclusion

After literature review of scientific researches related to these samples we may conclude that for the first time, detailed spectroscopic investigations have been performed using advanced spectroscopic techniques such as optical microscopy, WD-XRF, UVVis and FTIR Spectroscopy to study nematode infected roots to evaluate the changes in their chemical compositions in order to know the causes and changes that appear at the elemental and molecular level due to the attack of nematode. The results obtained in infected roots have been then compared with the healthy okra plants and significant changes also been observed. Mineral elements and heavy metal elements associated with nematode and fungal infections have been successfully identified and quantified in okra roots. Successfully, we have employed FTIR spectroscopy to study the changes in starch and protein contents in infected okra roots. Other chemical constituents such as carbohydrates, fatty acids, lipids, along with some moisture content have also been detected in okra roots. Significant changes in the absorption intensities of the peaks in healthy and infected okra roots have also been observed. Most importantly, our spectroscopic investigations will help in better understanding of the risks caused by nematodes for its further preventive strategies. The spectroscopic methods such as FTIR, UVVis and XRF can be used as a potential candidate to provide the significant and valuable information's in plant science to explain the role of the uptake of heavy metals and to determine the role of complex molecules in diseased plants.
